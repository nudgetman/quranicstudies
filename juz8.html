Surah 6
سُورَةُ الْأَنْعَامِ
Al-An'am — The Cattle
Makkan · 165 verses · Revealed in its entirety in one night

Al-An'am is one of the most theologically rich Surahs, revealed in its entirety at once. It confronts polytheism head-on, establishes the absolute Oneness of Allah, defends the prophethood of Muhammad ﷺ, and lays down ethical commandments. Juz 8 picks up from verse 111, midway through Allah's decisive arguments against disbelief.

Al-An'am 6:111–121
The Dead Heart vs. The Living Heart
وَلَوْ أَنَّنَا نَزَّلْنَا إِلَيْهِمُ الْمَلَائِكَةَ وَكَلَّمَهُمُ الْمَوْتَىٰ وَحَشَرْنَا عَلَيْهِمْ كُلَّ شَيْءٍ قُبُلًا مَا كَانُوا لِيُؤْمِنُوا إِلَّا أَن يَشَاءَ اللَّهُ
"And even if We had sent down to them the angels, and the dead had spoken to them, and We had gathered every thing before them, they would not have believed unless Allah willed." — 6:111
أَوَمَن كَانَ مَيْتًا فَأَحْيَيْنَاهُ وَجَعَلْنَا لَهُ نُورًا يَمْشِي بِهِ فِي النَّاسِ كَمَن مَّثَلُهُ فِي الظُّلُمَاتِ لَيْسَ بِخَارِجٍ مِّنْهَا
"Is the one who was dead and We gave him life, and We made for him a light by which to walk among the people, like the one who is in darkness from which he can never emerge?" — 6:122
These verses deliver a profound psychological and spiritual diagnosis. The disbelievers of Makkah demanded sign after sign — angels appearing, the dead speaking, miracles on demand — yet Allah declares that even if all of this had happened, their hearts would still not have accepted faith. Why? Because the obstacle was never lack of evidence; it was a heart sealed by arrogance, pride, and attachment to ancestral customs. Verse 122 then draws one of the Qur'an's most beautiful contrasts: the person who was spiritually dead — wandering in confusion, without purpose or moral clarity — and was then brought to life by faith and given divine light to navigate through the darkness of human society. Contrast this with the one whose entire existence remains trapped in layers of spiritual darkness. These are not merely two conditions; they are two entirely different modes of being.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir explains that this verse was revealed partly in response to the persistent demands of the Quraysh for supernatural signs. He notes that guidance is ultimately in Allah's hands — the human's role is sincerity and openness. He also cites the opinion that verse 122 refers specifically to 'Umar ibn al-Khattab (رضي الله عنه), who was in spiritual death before Islam and walked in the light of faith afterward, though the verse is general in application to every believer.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn interprets "dead" (mayyitan) as metaphorical spiritual death — being in a state of disbelief and heedlessness. The "light" (nūr) is faith and knowledge of the Qur'an, which illuminates the path in this life. The "darkness" from which none escapes is the condition of the unbeliever who has no guiding principle to distinguish right from wrong.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di draws from this passage a lesson about the limitless mercy of Allah: He does not withhold guidance because He lacks compassion, but because the one who hardens their heart against every sign has chosen their condition. Guidance is a gift from Allah, not earned by demand. The believer who walks in light should be profoundly grateful — their faith is a divine bestowal, not a personal achievement.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 🔒 Demanding miracles is often not a quest for truth — it is a way to delay accepting truth one has already rejected inwardly.
  • 💡 Faith is described as light (nūr) — it gives direction, clarity, and safety in a world full of moral confusion.
  • ❤️ Conversion to Islam is described as being brought from death to life — a complete spiritual rebirth.
  • 🤲 Gratitude for one's faith should be constant, since it is a gift from Allah, not something we produce ourselves.
  • 👁️ Signs are everywhere — what determines whether we see them is the condition of our heart, not the quantity of evidence.
Al-An'am 6:122–129
Satan's Deception & The Hierarchy of Wrong
وَكَذَٰلِكَ جَعَلْنَا لِكُلِّ نَبِيٍّ عَدُوًّا شَيَاطِينَ الْإِنسِ وَالْجِنِّ يُوحِي بَعْضُهُمْ إِلَىٰ بَعْضٍ زُخْرُفَ الْقَوْلِ غُرُورًا
"And thus We have made for every prophet an enemy — devils among mankind and jinn, inspiring one another with adorned speech as deception. But if your Lord had willed, they would not have done it, so leave them and what they invent." — 6:112
وَعَلَىٰ كُلِّ قَلْبِ مُتَكَبِّرٍ جَبَّارٍ
"…and He seals [the hearts of] every arrogant tyrant." — 6:124 (part)
Here the Qur'an lifts the curtain on a cosmic reality: every messenger throughout history faced coordinated opposition, not just from human enemies but from a network of jinn and human devils who share "adorned speech" — beautiful-sounding but hollow rhetoric designed to confuse and mislead. The Arabic phrase "zukhrufa al-qawl" (زُخْرُفَ الْقَوْلِ) is extraordinary — it means speech decorated like gold but hollow inside, like gilded words that catch the eye but have no substance. The passage then reveals the divine mechanism of hardening: it is not arbitrary. Allah seals the heart of the arrogant tyrant (mutakabbir jabbār) — one who has chosen pride and oppression as a lifestyle. This sealing is a consequence of the person's own sustained choice, not an injustice from Allah.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir notes that "shayāṭīn al-ins wal-jinn" (human and jinn devils) refers to those who actively work to spread misguidance. Humans can be devils just as much as jinn — through political manipulation, cultural pressure, and seductive falsehood. He cites hadith indicating that the Prophet ﷺ was warned that even among humans he would find his bitterest enemies.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn explains "zukhrufa al-qawl" as speech ornamented with falsehood to make it appealing — similar to how a jeweler decorates cheap metal with gold plating. The opposition to prophets is systematic and recurring, teaching us that the struggle between truth and falsehood is a constant feature of human history, not an accident.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di emphasizes the consolation in this passage for believers: when you face opposition, mockery, and sophisticated arguments against Islam, know this is the same experience every prophet faced. The pattern is ancient and predictable. True strength comes from recognizing this pattern and refusing to be dazzled by "adorned speech" that lacks divine foundation.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 🌐 Falsehood operates as a network — it has human and invisible architects working in coordination.
  • 🎭 "Adorned speech" is one of the most dangerous weapons against faith — eloquent, polished, but empty of truth.
  • 💪 The Prophet ﷺ was told to leave those who persist in deception — not every argument deserves exhausting engagement.
  • ⚖️ Arrogance and oppression close the heart to guidance — this is a spiritual law, as reliable as any physical law.
  • 📖 Opposition to truth is a historical constant, which means believers should expect it, not be surprised by it.
Al-An'am 6:130–140
Warning to Those Who Turn Away
يَا مَعْشَرَ الْجِنِّ وَالْإِنسِ أَلَمْ يَأْتِكُمْ رُسُلٌ مِّنكُمْ يَقُصُّونَ عَلَيْكُمْ آيَاتِي وَيُنذِرُونَكُمْ لِقَاءَ يَوْمِكُمْ هَٰذَا
"O company of jinn and mankind, did there not come to you messengers from among you, relating to you My verses and warning you of the meeting of this Day of yours?" — 6:130
This passage presents a dramatic courtroom scene set on the Day of Resurrection. Both jinn and humans are addressed together — a reminder that the moral and spiritual law applies to all conscious, accountable beings. The question is rhetorical: messengers did come, warnings were given, verses were recited. Every soul will acknowledge this. The passage then describes a devastating custom of the Arab polytheists: they invented religious prohibitions and invented religious permissions — declaring certain crops and animals sacred to their idols, female animals forbidden for men, newborns dedicated to their gods — all without any divine authority. Allah rebukes this as pure fabrication, and promises that those who invent religious law without knowledge will be held accountable.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir highlights that jinn and humans share the same fundamental test and accountability. The Qur'an records at several places that jinn also received and accepted prophetic guidance (as in Surah Al-Jinn). This verse reminds both groups that ignorance cannot be claimed as an excuse on the Day of Judgment, since the message reached all.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn notes the painful irony of those who invent religious rulings without authority: they believed they were serving their gods by these restrictions, yet they were in reality fabricating against Allah. The verse serves as a timeless warning against religious innovation that has no basis in revelation.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di sees in this passage a universal principle: "Every soul will confess on that Day." No one will deny that Allah's proofs were established. The Day of Judgment is not a debate — it is a final acknowledgment of what was always true. He urges believers to use this world to align with that truth voluntarily, before being forced to acknowledge it compulsorily.
✨ Key Lessons
  • ⚖️ Both jinn and humans are equally accountable — moral responsibility is not a uniquely human burden.
  • 📣 No one can claim they were never warned — messengers reached every community throughout history.
  • 🚫 Inventing religious rulings without divine authority is one of the gravest of wrongs in Islam.
  • 🗓️ The Day of Judgment involves a full acknowledgment — the guilty will confess there what they denied here.
  • 🌿 What Allah declared halal is clean and good; adding prohibitions or permissions without authority corrupts religion.
Al-An'am 6:141–150
Lawful Food & False Attribution to Allah
وَهُوَ الَّذِي أَنشَأَ جَنَّاتٍ مَّعْرُوشَاتٍ وَغَيْرَ مَعْرُوشَاتٍ وَالنَّخْلَ وَالزَّرْعَ مُخْتَلِفًا أُكُلُهُ وَالزَّيْتُونَ وَالرُّمَّانَ مُتَشَابِهًا وَغَيْرَ مُتَشَابِهٍ ۚ كُلُوا مِن ثَمَرِهِ إِذَا أَثْمَرَ وَآتُوا حَقَّهُ يَوْمَ حَصَادِهِ
"And it is He who produced gardens trellised and untrellised, and date palms, and crops of different food, and olives, and pomegranates, similar and dissimilar. Eat of its fruit when it yields and give its due on the day of its harvest." — 6:141
After exposing the false religious prohibitions of the polytheists, Allah now presents the truth: He is the Creator of all food, and He has made it lawful and good. The verse invites us to simply eat — with gratitude — and to give the poor their share on harvest day (the foundation of Zakat al-Zuru' in Islamic law). The list of fruits — trellised gardens, palms, olives, pomegranates — reads like a celebration of divine generosity. Notice that similar-looking fruits taste completely different, and different-looking fruits may taste similar. This diversity within unity points to a Creator whose artistry defies formula. Then comes the key command: what is truly prohibited is only what Allah has named (dead animals, blood, pork, and that slaughtered for other than Allah). Everything else remains the default — lawful and good.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir identifies "give its due on the day of harvest" as the basis for obligatory charity (zakah) on agricultural produce. He cites scholars who say one-tenth (ʿushr) is due for rain-irrigated crops, and one-twentieth for crops that require human irrigation effort. This verse thus connects our consumption directly to social responsibility.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn emphasizes that the Qur'anic baseline is permissibility: all food is halal unless explicitly prohibited. The Arabs had invented countless restrictions — this animal for men only, that animal for gods, this crop forbidden in certain months — all without authority. Allah sweeps these away and restores natural simplicity.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di notes the beautiful principle here: Allah the Creator knows best what is good and harmful for His creation. When He permits something, it is good. When He prohibits something, it is harmful. Human-invented prohibitions and permissions interfere with this divine wisdom and create unnecessary hardship or dangerous leniency — both of which harm society.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 🌴 The diversity of halal food is itself a mercy and a sign of Allah's creative generosity.
  • 💰 Giving the poor their share at harvest is not optional charity — it is a right (ḥaqq) they hold over our wealth.
  • 🍖 True halal and haram come only from Allah — human beings have no authority to create religious dietary laws.
  • 🌾 The default for food is permissibility — Islam is not a religion of excessive restriction.
  • 🐄 Wasting food is condemned — "do not waste, for He does not love the wasteful" follows in the very same passage (6:141).
Al-An'am 6:151–153
The Divine Commandments — The Wasiyyah of Allah
قُلْ تَعَالَوْا أَتْلُ مَا حَرَّمَ رَبُّكُمْ عَلَيْكُمْ ۖ أَلَّا تُشْرِكُوا بِهِ شَيْئًا ۖ وَبِالْوَالِدَيْنِ إِحْسَانًا ۖ وَلَا تَقْتُلُوا أَوْلَادَكُم مِّنْ إِمْلَاقٍ ۖ نَّحْنُ نَرْزُقُكُمْ وَإِيَّاهُمْ ۖ وَلَا تَقْرَبُوا الْفَوَاحِشَ مَا ظَهَرَ مِنْهَا وَمَا بَطَنَ ۖ وَلَا تَقْتُلُوا النَّفْسَ الَّتِي حَرَّمَ اللَّهُ إِلَّا بِالْحَقِّ
"Say: Come, I will recite what your Lord has prohibited to you. Join nothing with Him; be good to your parents; do not kill your children out of poverty — We will provide for you and them; and do not approach immoralities, whether apparent or hidden; and do not kill the soul which Allah has forbidden, except by right of law." — 6:151
وَلَا تَقْرَبُوا مَالَ الْيَتِيمِ إِلَّا بِالَّتِي هِيَ أَحْسَنُ حَتَّىٰ يَبْلُغَ أَشُدَّهُ ۖ وَأَوْفُوا الْكَيْلَ وَالْمِيزَانَ بِالْقِسْطِ ۖ وَإِذَا قُلْتُمْ فَاعْدِلُوا وَلَوْ كَانَ ذَا قُرْبَىٰ
"And do not approach the wealth of the orphan, except in the best way, until he reaches maturity. And give full measure and weight in justice. And when you speak, be just, even if a close relative is concerned." — 6:152
وَأَنَّ هَٰذَا صِرَاطِي مُسْتَقِيمًا فَاتَّبِعُوهُ ۖ وَلَا تَتَّبِعُوا السُّبُلَ فَتَفَرَّقَ بِكُمْ عَن سَبِيلِهِ
"And this is My path, straight, so follow it; and do not follow the other paths, for they will scatter you from His path." — 6:153
These three verses contain what Islamic scholars call "wasiyyat al-Qur'an" — the Qur'an's most comprehensive ethical charter. The Prophet ﷺ reportedly said that whoever desires to read the will (wasiyyah) that the Prophet of Allah sealed with his ring, should read these three verses. They can be seen as Islam's Ten Commandments — a universal moral foundation that transcends time, culture, and civilization. The commandments move from theology (no shirk) → family ethics (honor parents, protect children) → personal morality (no lewdness) → sanctity of life → economic justice (orphan's wealth, honest weights) → judicial fairness (speak justly even against family) → and conclude with the overarching principle: one straight path, and many deviating paths. The image of "one straight path" with many branching diversions is profoundly true of moral life: there is essentially one coherent way to be just, honest, and God-fearing, but there are thousands of ways to drift from it.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir records the narration of Ibn Mas'ud (رضي الله عنه) who said: "Whoever wishes to read the last testament of Muhammad ﷺ that bears his seal, let him read these three verses." He explains each prohibition carefully — shirk as the most fundamental violation, then the family ethics, then the prohibition of immorality "apparent or hidden," meaning both public sin and secret sin are equally prohibited.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn draws attention to the phrase "do not kill your children out of poverty — We will provide for you and them." This was a direct response to the pre-Islamic Arab practice of female infanticide, done out of fear of poverty and social shame. Allah's answer is not a lecture but a promise: I will provide. Trust Me, and stop killing.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di focuses on the final image — the straight path and the many divergent paths. He explains that the "other paths" (al-subul) represent every ideology, philosophy, and moral system that contradicts divine guidance. They all diverge from Allah's path and from each other. Only the Sunnah of Allah is coherent, internally consistent, and leads to human flourishing. He sees in these verses a complete civilization-building program.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 🕌 Tawhid (pure monotheism) is the foundation of all ethics — every other commandment flows from it.
  • 👨‍👩‍👧 Parental rights and children's rights are both divinely protected — Islamic ethics is family-centered.
  • 🏺 Orphans are a special trust — taking advantage of their weakness is one of the gravest wrongs in Islam.
  • ⚖️ Economic justice — honest weights and measures — is a moral obligation, not just a business practice.
  • 🗣️ Speaking justly even against loved ones is required — favoritism in testimony is a form of injustice.
  • 🛤️ There is one straight path and countless deviating ones — clarity of purpose protects from being scattered by false choices.
Al-An'am 6:154–157
The Book of Moses & No Excuse Remains
ثُمَّ آتَيْنَا مُوسَى الْكِتَابَ تَمَامًا عَلَى الَّذِي أَحْسَنَ وَتَفْصِيلًا لِّكُلِّ شَيْءٍ وَهُدًى وَرَحْمَةً لَّعَلَّهُمْ بِلِقَاءِ رَبِّهِمْ يُؤْمِنُونَ
"Then We gave Moses the Scripture, complete for the one who does good, and as a detailed explanation of all things, and as guidance and mercy, that perhaps they would believe in the meeting of their Lord." — 6:154
أَن تَقُولُوا إِنَّمَا أُنزِلَ الْكِتَابُ عَلَىٰ طَائِفَتَيْنِ مِن قَبْلِنَا وَإِن كُنَّا عَن دِرَاسَتِهِمْ لَغَافِلِينَ
"…lest you say, 'Scripture was only sent down to two groups before us, and we were indeed unaware of their study.'" — 6:156
Here the Qur'an establishes its own necessity by anticipating every excuse. The Arabs might say: "We were never given a scripture." "We couldn't understand the Torah — it was for Jews." "We couldn't understand the Gospel — it was for Christians." The Qur'an's response: now you have a book in your own language, Arabic, clear and direct, so no such excuse remains. The reference to the Book of Moses serves a double purpose: it validates the prophetic chain (Moses received revelation just as Muhammad ﷺ did), and it reminds the Arabs that divine books have always been complete, detailed, and mercy — not burdens. The Qur'an continues this tradition.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir explains that these verses address three groups simultaneously: the Arabs who had no previous scripture, the Jews who had the Torah, and the Christians who had the Gospel. Each group is given what applies to them. For the Arabs, the Qur'an closes every door of excuse — it came to them in their language, in their own time, through one of their own people.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn notes "tammān ʿalā alladhī aḥsana" — the Torah was given as completion/perfection for those who do good. Some scholars read this as: the Torah fulfills and rewards the one who acts righteously. The Qur'an uses this reference to affirm continuity: divine books share the same purpose across all nations.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di emphasizes the closing of excuses as a theme of divine justice. Allah does not punish without first establishing proof. By sending the Qur'an to the Arabs in Arabic, all claims of ignorance are nullified. This is why the Qur'an repeatedly describes itself as "mubīn" (clear) — its clarity is an act of justice, not merely a stylistic quality.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 📚 Divine books across all prophets share one mission: guidance, mercy, and preparation for meeting Allah.
  • 🌍 The Qur'an was sent in Arabic to the Arabs specifically, closing every avenue of excuse for them.
  • 🔗 The Qur'an confirms and completes previous revelations — it does not erase them, it restores their pure message.
  • ⚖️ Allah never punishes a people without first establishing proof through a messenger or scripture.
  • 🙏 "Perhaps they would believe in meeting their Lord" — the entire project of prophethood is oriented toward the Afterlife.
Al-An'am 6:158–165
The Day of Signs & Vicegerency on Earth
هَلْ يَنظُرُونَ إِلَّا أَن تَأْتِيَهُمُ الْمَلَائِكَةُ أَوْ يَأْتِيَ رَبُّكَ أَوْ يَأْتِيَ بَعْضُ آيَاتِ رَبِّكَ ۗ يَوْمَ يَأْتِي بَعْضُ آيَاتِ رَبِّكَ لَا يَنفَعُ نَفْسًا إِيمَانُهَا لَمْ تَكُنْ آمَنَتْ مِن قَبْلُ
"Are they waiting only for the angels to come to them, or for your Lord to come, or for some of the signs of your Lord? The day some of the signs of your Lord will come, belief will not benefit a soul that did not believe before." — 6:158
وَهُوَ الَّذِي جَعَلَكُمْ خَلَائِفَ الْأَرْضِ وَرَفَعَ بَعْضَكُمْ فَوْقَ بَعْضٍ دَرَجَاتٍ لِّيَبْلُوَكُمْ فِي مَا آتَاكُمْ
"And it is He who has made you successors/vicegerents upon the earth and has raised some of you above others in degrees, to test you through what He has given you." — 6:165
The Surah closes with two powerful ideas. First: there is a point of no return — when the great signs appear before the Day of Judgment (the rising of the sun from the west, the emergence of the Beast), faith professed at that moment will have no value. The window for meaningful faith is now, while it is still a choice made in the unseen. This creates urgency: do not delay your spiritual reckoning. Second: the profound concept of khilāfah (vicegerency). Humanity has been appointed as stewards of the earth — not owners, not masters, but caretakers on behalf of the true Owner, Allah. And differences in wealth, status, and ability among people are deliberate: Allah tests the wealthy with their wealth, the powerful with their power, the knowledgeable with their knowledge. No position is permanent; all are tests.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir cites multiple hadith about the "minor signs" vs "major signs" of the Hour. He explains that the verse specifically refers to the major signs — such as the sunrise from the west — after which repentance is closed. Until then, the door remains open. He emphasizes this as one of the most important calls to urgency in the Qur'an: "Act now while there is time."
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
On verse 165, Al-Jalalayn explains that "raised some above others in degrees" refers to differences in rizq (provision), health, intellect, and social position — all of which are divine tests. The wealthy person is tested in whether they are grateful and charitable; the poor person is tested in whether they are patient and trusting in Allah.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di closes his commentary on this Surah with reflection on the concept of khilāfah: humanity's role as stewards carries enormous responsibility. We do not own the earth or its resources — we are trustees. This reframes how we interact with the environment, with wealth, and with power. Everything we "have" is on loan from Allah, and we will be asked about it.
✨ Key Lessons
  • ⏰ The window for meaningful faith and repentance is this life — it closes at a specific moment we cannot predict.
  • 🌅 The major signs of the Day of Judgment will remove all ambiguity — but belief at that point will have no merit.
  • 🌍 Humans are khalīfah (stewards) of the earth — we hold it in trust for Allah, not as personal property.
  • 💎 Differences in wealth and status are deliberate divine tests — how we handle our position defines our character.
  • 🤲 Unity is stressed in the final verse — fragmentation into sects that rejoice in their own divisions is condemned.

Surah 7
سُورَةُ الْأَعْرَافِ
Al-A'raf — The Heights
Makkan · 206 verses · The longest Makkan Surah

Al-A'raf takes its name from "The Heights" — the barrier between Paradise and Hell from which some souls will watch. It is the longest Makkan Surah, rich with the stories of previous prophets and their struggles. Juz 8 covers its opening through the stories of Nuh, Hud, Salih, Lut, and Shu'ayb — a panoramic gallery of divine mercy and human accountability.

Al-A'raf 7:1–10
The Muqatta'at & The Weighing of Deeds
المص
"Alif, Lam, Meem, Saad." — 7:1
وَالْوَزْنُ يَوْمَئِذٍ الْحَقُّ ۚ فَمَن ثَقُلَتْ مَوَازِينُهُ فَأُولَٰئِكَ هُمُ الْمُفْلِحُونَ ۝ وَمَنْ خَفَّتْ مَوَازِينُهُ فَأُولَٰئِكَ الَّذِينَ خَسِرُوا أَنفُسَهُم
"And the weighing on that Day will be the truth. Those whose scales are heavy — they are the successful. And those whose scales are light — those are the ones who have lost their souls." — 7:8–9
The Surah opens with the mysterious letters (Muqatta'at) — ALMS — which scholars have understood variously: as attention-grabbing devices, as hints at the miraculous nature of the Qur'an composed from ordinary Arabic letters, or as names whose full meaning only Allah knows. These letters appear at the opening of 29 Surahs and remain among the most discussed phenomena in Qur'anic studies. Then the Surah moves immediately to the scales of the Day of Judgment. This is a real, physical weighing that will occur. The deeds of a person's entire life will be weighed — with absolute precision and perfect justice. No one can manipulate these scales. No one can add weight to them by influence or wealth. This image of cosmic justice is one of the most stabilizing beliefs in Islam: no good deed, however small, is ever truly lost.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
On the Muqatta'at, Ibn Kathir adopts the position that their true meaning is known only to Allah — they are part of the mutashābihāt (ambiguous verses). On the scales (mawāzīn), he affirms that these are literal, real scales on which deeds — or the records of deeds — will be weighed. He cites the hadith of the person whose paper card bearing "Lā ilāha illallāh" outweighs 99 scrolls of sins.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn explains "khafat mawāzīnuhu" (whose scales are light) as referring to those whose good deeds are outweighed by their evil deeds. He notes the powerful phrase "khasirū anfusahum" — they have lost their own selves. The most total loss is not financial or social; it is the loss of one's soul — its purpose, its potential, its eternal destiny.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di meditates on "al-waznu yawma'idhin al-ḥaqq" — the weighing on that Day is the Truth. He explains: in this world, scales can be falsified, power can distort justice, and the innocent can be condemned. But on that Day, the scale is divine. The word al-ḥaqq (the Truth/the Right/the Real) indicates that this weighing is perfect, impartial, and absolute. No appeal lies beyond it.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 🔡 The Muqatta'at remind us that the Qur'an contains elements beyond complete human comprehension — a sign of its divine origin.
  • ⚖️ Divine justice on the Day of Judgment is the ultimate guarantee — no wrong goes unpunished, no good goes unrecorded.
  • 💫 A single sincere deed can tip the scales — the hadith of the card with Lā ilāha illallāh teaches that quality matters as much as quantity.
  • 😔 The phrase "lost their souls" is the Qur'an's definition of the ultimate failure — worse than any worldly loss.
  • 🌱 Every small good deed matters — the scales are infinitely precise.
Al-A'raf 7:11–25
Adam, Iblis & Humanity's Primordial Test
وَلَقَدْ خَلَقْنَاكُمْ ثُمَّ صَوَّرْنَاكُمْ ثُمَّ قُلْنَا لِلْمَلَائِكَةِ اسْجُدُوا لِآدَمَ فَسَجَدُوا إِلَّا إِبْلِيسَ لَمْ يَكُن مِّنَ السَّاجِدِينَ
"We created you, then We formed you, then We said to the angels: 'Prostrate to Adam' — and they prostrated, except Iblis; he was not among those who prostrated." — 7:11
قَالَ مَا مَنَعَكَ أَلَّا تَسْجُدَ إِذْ أَمَرْتُكَ ۖ قَالَ أَنَا خَيْرٌ مِّنْهُ خَلَقْتَنِي مِن نَّارٍ وَخَلَقْتَهُ مِن طِينٍ
"Allah said: 'What prevented you from prostrating when I commanded you?' He said: 'I am better than him. You created me from fire and You created him from clay.'" — 7:12
فَبِمَا أَغْوَيْتَنِي لَأَقْعُدَنَّ لَهُمْ صِرَاطَكَ الْمُسْتَقِيمَ
"He said: 'Because You have put me in error, I will surely sit in wait for them on Your straight path.'" — 7:16
This is one of the most pivotal narratives in the entire Qur'an — the story of the cosmic fall that defines the human condition. Iblis's sin was not ignorance but arrogance. He knew the command, understood it, and chose to disobey based on a self-constructed argument: "I am better than him — fire versus clay." This is rationalized disobedience, the most dangerous form of sin. Notice how Iblis's response to being expelled is not remorse but resentment — immediately he declares war on humanity, vowing to lie in ambush on the very straight path of Allah. His strategy is named: he will attack from every direction (front, back, left, right), trying to make the majority of humanity ungrateful. The story of Adam and Eve follows: they were deceived, they ate from the forbidden tree, felt shame, and immediately sought forgiveness. Allah forgave them and then sent them to earth — not as punishment alone, but with a promise: "Whoever follows My guidance shall neither fear nor grieve." Earth is not exile; it is a theater for return.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir draws a profound lesson from Iblis's use of comparative reasoning to justify disobedience. He warns that any time a human uses their intellect to construct arguments for why they should not follow a divine command, they are mirroring Iblis's foundational error. Obedience to Allah does not require understanding; it requires submission. The first scholar to use analogy (qiyās) to contradict a divine text was Iblis himself.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn explains that when Adam and Hawa felt shame (7:22), they immediately sought to cover themselves with leaves from Paradise — the first act of modesty, the first instinct of human dignity. This instinct for modesty is presented as natural and primordial, not as a social convention. Allah then addressed them: "Did I not forbid you this tree?" — an accountability moment that prefigures the Day of Judgment.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di contrasts the responses of Iblis and Adam beautifully. Iblis sinned and doubled down with pride and blame ("because You misled me"). Adam sinned and immediately turned back with humility ("Our Lord, we have wronged ourselves"). The same scenario — disobedience — leads to opposite outcomes based purely on the character of the response. This is the template for all human repentance: acknowledge the wrong, take responsibility, ask for mercy.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 🎭 Arrogance was Iblis's original sin — the same pride that says "I know better than Allah's command" lives within every human.
  • 🔥 Using logical arguments to override divine commands is a trap of Iblis, not a sign of intellect.
  • 🌿 Shame and modesty are primordial human instincts — they emerged before any culture or civilization.
  • 🙏 Adam and Hawa's response to sin — immediate humility and repentance — is the model for every believer.
  • 🌍 Earth is not a punishment but a proving ground — the promise of guidance means every human can find the way home.
  • 👁️ Iblis attacks from every direction — vigilance in all aspects of life is required, not just in obvious moral decisions.
Al-A'raf 7:26–43
Children of Adam & The Gates of Paradise
يَا بَنِي آدَمَ قَدْ أَنزَلْنَا عَلَيْكُمْ لِبَاسًا يُوَارِي سَوْآتِكُمْ وَرِيشًا ۖ وَلِبَاسُ التَّقْوَىٰ ذَٰلِكَ خَيْرٌ
"O children of Adam, We have bestowed upon you clothing to conceal your private parts and as adornment. But the garment of righteousness — that is best." — 7:26
وَنَادَىٰ أَصْحَابُ الْجَنَّةِ أَصْحَابَ النَّارِ أَن قَدْ وَجَدْنَا مَا وَعَدَنَا رَبُّنَا حَقًّا فَهَلْ وَجَدتُّم مَّا وَعَدَ رَبُّكُمْ حَقًّا
"And the companions of Paradise will call out to the companions of the Fire: 'We have found what our Lord promised us to be true — did you find what your Lord promised to be true?'" — 7:44
The passage opens with a triple gift to humanity: physical clothing, adornment (beauty), and most importantly, "the garment of taqwa." This is one of the most poetic expressions in the Qur'an — righteousness as clothing. Just as a garment covers vulnerability, protects from elements, and dignifies appearance, taqwa (God-consciousness) protects the soul from sin, shields from spiritual harm, and gives the believer dignity before Allah. The passage then describes the opening of Paradise. Groups of believers will enter, gate by gate, and the angels will greet them with "Peace be upon you — you have been good, so enter to abide eternally." The companions of Paradise and the companions of the Fire are then portrayed in a haunting exchange across the barrier — a moment of ultimate reality where every worldly claim is tested against eternal truth.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir explains "libās al-taqwā" (garment of taqwa) as the fear of Allah that shields the heart from sin. He draws the analogy: just as clothing protects the body from heat, cold, and harm, taqwa protects the soul. He also discusses the eight gates of Paradise, each corresponding to different acts of worship (prayer, fasting, charity, jihad, etc.).
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn notes that the dialogue between the people of Paradise and the people of the Fire is not for their benefit — the Fire's inhabitants cannot leave — but serves as a final confirmation of divine truth. The question "did you find Allah's promise true?" expects only one answer. Every soul, in the end, will acknowledge that everything Allah promised was exactly as He said.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di reflects on the angels' greeting at the gates of Paradise: "Salāmun ʿalaykum ṭibtum fadhkhulūhā khālidīn" — "Peace upon you, you have been pure/good, so enter eternally." He notes the word "ṭibtum" — it means not only "you did good" but "you were pure in your nature." The believer's internal state — sincerity, humility, love of Allah — matters as much as their outward actions.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 👘 Taqwa is the most important garment — the one that matters in the next world, not the one that impresses people in this one.
  • 🚪 Paradise has gates — and there are different paths of righteousness that lead to different entrances.
  • 😇 The angels' greeting at Paradise's gates — "Peace upon you" — shows that even entry into Paradise begins with divine kindness.
  • 🔥 The conversation between Paradise and the Fire is a final affirmation — everything Allah promised came true.
  • 💎 Internal purity (ṭayyib) is as important as external good deeds — the two must align.
Al-A'raf 7:44–53
The Great Dialogue Across the Veil
وَبَيْنَهُمَا حِجَابٌ ۚ وَعَلَى الْأَعْرَافِ رِجَالٌ يَعْرِفُونَ كُلًّا بِسِيمَاهُمْ ۚ وَنَادَوْا أَصْحَابَ الْجَنَّةِ أَن سَلَامٌ عَلَيْكُمْ ۚ لَمْ يَدْخُلُوهَا وَهُمْ يَطْمَعُونَ
"And between them is a barrier, and on the Heights (Al-A'raf) are men who recognize each by their mark. And they call to the companions of Paradise: 'Peace be upon you.' They have not yet entered it, but they aspire." — 7:46
This passage introduces one of the most mysterious figures in Qur'anic eschatology: the People of the A'raf — the Heights. They stand on an elevated partition between Paradise and the Fire, able to see both. Scholars have differed on who they are: some say those whose good and evil deeds are perfectly equal; others say the prophets, or the martyrs, or great scholars watching over the proceedings of the Day of Judgment. These people recognize the inhabitants of both destinations by their distinguishing marks (simāhum) — the people of Paradise are illuminated with light and joy; the people of the Fire are darkened. The people of the A'raf call greetings to the people of Paradise, longing to join them, while warning the people of the Fire that their numbers, wealth, and arrogance counted for nothing.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir presents the most widely held view: the People of the A'raf are those whose good and bad deeds are equal, so they cannot be assigned to either abode initially. They will ultimately enter Paradise through Allah's mercy after a period of waiting. He cites this as an evidence for the Qur'an's nuanced view of divine justice — it is not simply binary but contains gradations and moments of divine mercy.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn focuses on the psychological state of the People of the A'raf: "lam yadkhulūhā wa hum yaṭmaʿūn" — they have not entered Paradise yet, but they long and hope. This hope (ṭamaʿ) is not futile — it represents the mercy of Allah that keeps the door of hope open even in that transitional state. They will ultimately enter Paradise, inshAllah.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di draws a contemporary lesson: the people of the A'raf who call to those destined for the Fire — "Your numbers did not protect you, nor did your arrogance" — serve as witnesses and reminders. In this world, we sometimes think that having many people agree with us, or having wealth and status, validates our choices. This passage shows that divine truth is independent of consensus or material success.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 🏔️ Al-A'raf reveals that the Afterlife has more nuance than a simple binary — there are intermediate states and divine wisdom at work.
  • 👁️ People will be recognized on the Day of Judgment by the light or darkness of their deeds — our actions leave marks on the soul.
  • 💫 Hope is valid even in the most uncertain situations — the People of the A'raf still hope, and that hope will be fulfilled.
  • 💰 Wealth, numbers, and social prestige offer zero protection on the Day of Judgment.
  • ⚖️ Allah's justice accounts for every gradation of human moral standing — no one is simply dumped into a category without consideration.
Al-A'raf 7:54–58
The Lord of Creation & The Gift of Rain
إِنَّ رَبَّكُمُ اللَّهُ الَّذِي خَلَقَ السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضَ فِي سِتَّةِ أَيَّامٍ ثُمَّ اسْتَوَىٰ عَلَى الْعَرْشِ يُغْشِي اللَّيْلَ النَّهَارَ يَطْلُبُهُ حَثِيثًا
"Indeed, your Lord is Allah, who created the heavens and the earth in six days and then established Himself above the Throne. He covers the night with the day — pursuing it swiftly." — 7:54
وَهُوَ الَّذِي يُرْسِلُ الرِّيَاحَ بُشْرًا بَيْنَ يَدَيْ رَحْمَتِهِ ۖ حَتَّىٰ إِذَا أَقَلَّتْ سَحَابًا ثِقَالًا سُقْنَاهُ لِبَلَدٍ مَّيِّتٍ فَأَنزَلْنَا بِهِ الْمَاءَ
"And it is He who sends the winds as good tidings before His mercy, until when they have carried heavy rainclouds, We drive them to a dead land, and We send down rain therein." — 7:57
After the eschatological drama of Paradise, the Fire, and the A'raf, the Surah pivots to a meditation on creation — grounding abstract theological concepts in the tangible world. Allah created the heavens and earth in six days (ayyām — periods/epochs, not necessarily 24-hour days) and established Himself above the Throne (istawā ʿalā al-ʿarsh) — an attribute affirmed without asking "how," in the manner of Ahl al-Sunnah. The wind-and-rain passage is breathtaking: winds are sent as "bushrā" — good news, heralds of mercy. Before the rain comes, the wind announces it. Then the heavy clouds are driven to dead, parched land, and water descends to bring vegetation to life. This is then explicitly compared to the descent of revelation to dead hearts — the Qur'an is Allah's rain for the soul.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir on "istawā ʿalā al-ʿarsh" follows Imam Malik's famous response: "Establishment is known, the 'how' is unknown, believing in it is obligatory, and asking about it is an innovation." He then shifts to the creation metaphor: just as rain revives dead land, the Qur'an revives dead hearts. He cites the Prophet ﷺ who compared a scholar who shares knowledge to rain — wherever it falls, it brings life.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn focuses on "bushrā" (good tidings) as a description of winds: they carry the announcement of rain before rain arrives. He sees this as a sign of Allah's kindness — He does not send mercy without first sending a sign. This parallels the prophets: before punishment, warnings came; before paradise, glad tidings were given.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di draws the full metaphor: "The good land — its vegetation springs forth with permission of its Lord. And the bad land — nothing comes from it but sparse vegetation." He explains this applies to hearts: the heart that is ready for guidance accepts the Qur'an and flourishes; the hardened heart hears the same words and produces nothing. The difference is in the receiver, not in the water.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 🌧️ Rain is described as divine mercy (raḥma) — even natural phenomena reflect Allah's care for creation.
  • 💨 Winds are sent as good news before rain — divine mercy is always announced before it arrives.
  • 🌱 The Qur'an is Allah's rain for the soul — it revives spiritually dead hearts just as water revives dead earth.
  • 🕌 Believers should call to Allah humbly and in secret (7:55) — loud, performative worship misses the spirit of du'a.
  • 🌍 The six "days" of creation point to a deliberate, measured creative process — not accident or chaos.
Al-A'raf 7:59–64
Prophet Nuh (Noah) and His People
لَقَدْ أَرْسَلْنَا نُوحًا إِلَىٰ قَوْمِهِ فَقَالَ يَا قَوْمِ اعْبُدُوا اللَّهَ مَا لَكُم مِّنْ إِلَٰهٍ غَيْرُهُ إِنِّي أَخَافُ عَلَيْكُمْ عَذَابَ يَوْمٍ عَظِيمٍ
"We sent Noah to his people, and he said: 'O my people, worship Allah; you have no deity other than Him. Indeed, I fear for you the punishment of a tremendous Day.'" — 7:59
قَالَ الْمَلَأُ مِن قَوْمِهِ إِنَّا لَنَرَاكَ فِي ضَلَالٍ مُّبِينٍ
"The chieftains of his people said: 'Indeed, we see you in clear error.'" — 7:60
The prophetic narratives of Al-A'raf begin with Nuh (Noah) — the first major prophet sent to a people who had fallen into polytheism after the righteous generation of Adam. Nuh's message was identical to every prophet's: "Worship Allah alone." And the opposition was identical: the elite (al-mala' — the chiefs and nobles) accused him of being misguided, foolish, and of ulterior motives. Nuh's response to being called misguided is a masterclass in prophetic wisdom: "I am not in error — I am a clear warner from the Lord of the Worlds. I convey my Lord's messages, I advise you sincerely, and I know from Allah what you do not know." Notice how Nuh does not become defensive or insulted. He simply states his credentials and mission clearly. After 950 years of calling (referenced elsewhere in the Qur'an), only a small number believed. The flood came and the disbelievers were drowned. The Ark carried the believers to safety — a physical salvation that parallels the spiritual salvation offered by every prophet.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir notes that Nuh is described in the Qur'an as ʿabdan shakūrā — an incredibly grateful servant. He persisted in calling his people for 950 years — an incomprehensible duration of patience. He emphasizes that Nuh's example is the greatest lesson in prophetic patience: the prophet's duty is to convey the message, not to guarantee results. Success belongs to Allah alone.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn explains "al-mala'" as the wealthy aristocratic class who led the opposition to every prophet. Their objection was largely social: following a prophet meant admitting that their ancestors were wrong, losing their religious leadership, and accepting the equality of the poor with the wealthy in the eyes of Allah — all deeply threatening to their status.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di points to Nuh's phrase: "I know from Allah what you do not know." This is not arrogance but the confidence of revelation. The prophet has access to divine knowledge — about the nature of truth, the reality of the Hereafter, the consequences of choices — that those without revelation cannot access. This is why following prophets is rational, not blind.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 🕰️ Prophet Nuh called his people for 950 years — patience in the face of rejection is a prophetic virtue, not optional.
  • 👑 The elite (al-mala') opposed every prophet — power and wealth often create the most resistance to spiritual truth.
  • 📢 The prophet's role is to convey and advise sincerely — results are in Allah's hands.
  • ⛵ The Ark is the original image of community-based salvation — faith is not just individual, it is communal.
  • 🌊 Divine punishment is the last resort after exhausting every warning — Allah's justice is always preceded by mercy.
Al-A'raf 7:65–72
Prophet Hud and the 'Aad
وَإِلَىٰ عَادٍ أَخَاهُمْ هُودًا ۗ قَالَ يَا قَوْمِ اعْبُدُوا اللَّهَ مَا لَكُم مِّنْ إِلَٰهٍ غَيْرُهُ ۖ أَفَلَا تَتَّقُونَ
"And to the 'Aad [We sent] their brother Hud. He said: 'O my people, worship Allah; you have no deity other than Him. Will you not fear Allah?'" — 7:65
قَالُوا أَجِئْتَنَا لِنَعْبُدَ اللَّهَ وَحْدَهُ وَنَذَرَ مَا كَانَ يَعْبُدُ آبَاؤُنَا
"They said: 'Have you come to us so that we would worship Allah alone and abandon what our fathers used to worship?'" — 7:70
The 'Aad were a powerful, physically imposing people of ancient Arabia, known for their tall buildings and great strength. They were immensely proud of their power. Their prophet Hud was one of them — "their brother" — sent to call them back to tawhid. The repeated phrase in these prophetic narratives — "O my people, worship Allah, you have no deity other than Him" — is the unchanging core message of all prophets. The 'Aad's response reveals the deepest resistance to prophetic calls: ancestral tradition. "Our fathers worshipped these idols" — this argument has been used to reject truth in every generation. Hud challenged them directly: are you going to follow your ancestors even when they were on the wrong path? A devastating wind (rīḥ ṣarṣar) was sent against the 'Aad, which continued for seven nights and eight days. The Qur'an records that not one person survived — only those who believed with Hud were saved.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir documents that the 'Aad were famous for their physical stature — described in traditions as enormously tall. Their pride in physical power made them feel invincible: "Who is greater than us in strength?" (41:15). Hud's response — "Allah, who created you, is greater" — exposes the fundamental category error: confusing created power with the power of the Creator.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn highlights Hud's argument: "I only rely on Allah for my sustenance. Are you going to deny the obvious blessing of your Lord?" This is an appeal to gratitude. The 'Aad owed their very strength, health, and prosperity to Allah — yet they attributed none of it to Him and used that very strength to resist His messenger.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di emphasizes that the wind was "musakhkharan ʿalayhim — brought under divine command and control against them." The very element of nature that had been their companion — wind, climate, weather — became their destruction. This pattern of divine justice repeats throughout history: the tools of human pride become the instruments of divine correction.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 💪 Physical power is no protection against divine will — the mightiest civilization in history was destroyed by wind.
  • 👴 "Our fathers did it" is not a valid argument for continuing wrong — tradition must be evaluated against truth, not used to block it.
  • 🌬️ The 'Aad's very element — wind and weather in their desert civilization — became their punishment: a powerful image of poetic divine justice.
  • 🤝 Every prophet was described as a "brother" to their people — prophethood is not distant and foreign but intimate and internal to communities.
  • 📖 Ingratitude for blessings is itself a form of shirk — attributing good to yourself rather than to Allah.
Al-A'raf 7:73–79
Prophet Salih and the She-Camel of Allah
وَإِلَىٰ ثَمُودَ أَخَاهُمْ صَالِحًا ۗ قَالَ يَا قَوْمِ اعْبُدُوا اللَّهَ مَا لَكُم مِّنْ إِلَٰهٍ غَيْرُهُ ۖ قَدْ جَاءَتْكُم بَيِّنَةٌ مِّن رَّبِّكُمْ ۖ هَٰذِهِ نَاقَةُ اللَّهِ لَكُمْ آيَةً
"And to the Thamud [We sent] their brother Salih. He said: 'O my people, worship Allah; you have no deity other than Him. There has come to you clear evidence from your Lord. This is the she-camel of Allah — a sign for you.'" — 7:73
فَعَقَرُوا النَّاقَةَ وَعَتَوْا عَنْ أَمْرِ رَبِّهِمْ وَقَالُوا يَا صَالِحُ ائْتِنَا بِمَا تَعِدُنَا إِن كُنتَ مِنَ الْمُرْسَلِينَ
"But they hamstrung the she-camel and defied the command of their Lord and said: 'O Salih, bring us what you promised us if you are of the messengers.'" — 7:77
The Thamud were the people of Prophet Salih, who carved their homes from mountains in the valley of Al-Hijr (referenced in the Qur'an and confirmed by archaeology in modern-day Saudi Arabia as the ancient Nabataean city of Mada'in Salih). They received a miraculous sign — a she-camel of divine origin that emerged from a rock and provided abundant milk. The condition was simple: let her graze freely and drink from the water. The camel was a test of whether the Thamud would respect a divine limit when they had the power to transgress it. They failed: nine men led the charge to kill her, provoking Salih's warning that in three days, punishment would come. They mocked him and dared him to bring it sooner. The earthquake (ṣayḥa — the cry/blast) came on the fourth day. It was so devastating that by morning, they lay prostrate in their homes — dead, as if they had never existed. Salih's farewell to his people is heartbreaking: "O my people, I conveyed my Lord's message to you and advised you — but you do not love those who advise."
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir identifies the nine men who planned the killing of the camel (7:48 in Surah An-Naml gives the number). He notes that the camel was not just a miraculous sign but a social test: her grazing and drinking displaced some of the tribe's resources, requiring them to share water one day with the camel and keep it for themselves the next. Their refusal to accept even this minor inconvenience for the sake of Allah's command revealed the depth of their arrogance.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn explains the phrase "faʿatawtum" — they acted with extreme insolence and transgression. The root ʿatā carries the sense of going well beyond all bounds. The camel was described as "nāqat Allāh" — the she-camel of Allah. Attacking Allah's sign was tantamount to attacking Allah's authority directly. The three-day warning was itself a mercy — a final chance to repent.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di focuses on the economic dimension of the test. The she-camel had rights over the land — a divine allocation of resources. The Thamud's killing of her represented not just religious defiance but a rejection of the concept that Allah has the right to allocate and restrict worldly resources. This connects to broader themes of halal, haram, and the authority of divine legislation over human economic desire.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 🐪 Divine signs are tests, not guarantees — receiving a miracle does not protect you if you respond with arrogance.
  • ⏳ The three-day warning shows Allah's patience and mercy even with those who have crossed lines — repentance is still possible until the end.
  • 💀 "As if they had never existed" — civilizations that reject prophets leave behind only ruins, not legacies.
  • 🤝 Salih's grief over his people shows that prophets love their communities deeply — rejection does not turn their hearts to hatred.
  • 🏔️ Archaeological evidence at Mada'in Salih (Al-Hijr) today confirms the Thamud's historical reality — the Qur'an's narratives are rooted in real history.
Al-A'raf 7:80–84
Prophet Lut and the People of Sodom
وَلُوطًا إِذْ قَالَ لِقَوْمِهِ أَتَأْتُونَ الْفَاحِشَةَ مَا سَبَقَكُم بِهَا مِنْ أَحَدٍ مِّنَ الْعَالَمِينَ
"And [remember] Lot, when he said to his people: 'Do you commit such immorality as no one has preceded you with in all the worlds?'" — 7:80
وَمَا كَانَ جَوَابَ قَوْمِهِ إِلَّا أَن قَالُوا أَخْرِجُوهُم مِّن قَرْيَتِكُمْ ۖ إِنَّهُمْ أُنَاسٌ يَتَطَهَّرُونَ
"And the answer of his people was not but that they said: 'Expel them from your city; indeed they are men who keep themselves pure.'" — 7:82
Prophet Lut was the nephew of Ibrahim (Abraham) and was sent to the people of Sodom, who had developed the novel practice of male homosexual relations as a cultural norm. The Qur'an is explicit: this was a moral corruption unprecedented in human history — "mā sabaqakum bihā min aḥad min al-ʿālamīn." Lut's call was to abandon this practice, which they rejected violently. The people of Sodom's response to Lut reveals the perversity of a society that has normalized immorality: they mocked the righteous for their purity. "Expel those who want to keep themselves clean" — virtue became offensive to them. This inversion of moral values is described elsewhere in the Qur'an as one of the signs of a deeply corrupted community. Three angels came disguised as handsome young men, which the people of Sodom tried to access. Lut was distressed. The angels revealed themselves and told Lut: take your family and leave tonight, do not look back. His wife, who sympathized with the townspeople, looked back and was destroyed with them. The town was turned upside down (juʿilnā ʿāliyahā sāfilahā) and rained upon with stones of sijjīl.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir records that the town of Sodom is believed to have existed in the area now covered by the Dead Sea (Bahr Lut — the Sea of Lut). He emphasizes the divine condemnation is absolute and clear, and draws on multiple Qur'anic verses and hadith to explain the severity of this particular sin in Islam — it represents a rejection of the natural order built into human creation.
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn notes the bitterly ironic accusation against Lut and his followers: "they are men who keep themselves pure (yataṭahharūn)." This was meant as mockery, but it is actually their highest praise. Being mocked for righteousness in a corrupt society is, in the Qur'an's framing, a badge of honor — exactly as the early Muslims were mocked in Makkah.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di focuses on Lut's wife as a warning about domestic compromise: she was in Lut's household but her heart was with the townspeople. He uses this to teach that proximity to righteousness — even in the home of a prophet — does not automatically produce righteousness. The heart's orientation is what determines one's ultimate fate.
✨ Key Lessons
  • 🔄 When a society normalizes immorality, the virtuous become the outsiders — this pattern is ancient, not modern.
  • 🏡 Even being in a prophet's household does not guarantee salvation — it is the heart's sincere alignment that matters.
  • 👼 The angels' visit to Lut shows the divine care for the righteous: before destruction, the believers were guided to safety.
  • ⚠️ "Do not look back" — sometimes obedience requires cutting ties completely with what is being left behind.
  • 📍 The ruins of destroyed civilizations are described by Allah as "clear signs" for those who travel past them and reflect.
Al-A'raf 7:85–87
Prophet Shu'ayb and the People of Madyan
وَإِلَىٰ مَدْيَنَ أَخَاهُمْ شُعَيْبًا ۗ قَالَ يَا قَوْمِ اعْبُدُوا اللَّهَ مَا لَكُم مِّنْ إِلَٰهٍ غَيْرُهُ ۖ قَدْ جَاءَتْكُم بَيِّنَةٌ مِّن رَّبِّكُمْ ۖ فَأَوْفُوا الْكَيْلَ وَالْمِيزَانَ وَلَا تَبْخَسُوا النَّاسَ أَشْيَاءَهُمْ
"And to Madyan [We sent] their brother Shu'ayb. He said: 'O my people, worship Allah; you have no deity other than Him. There has come to you clear evidence from your Lord. So give full measure and weight and do not diminish people's goods.'" — 7:85
وَلَا تَقْعُدُوا بِكُلِّ صِرَاطٍ تُوعِدُونَ وَتَصُدُّونَ عَن سَبِيلِ اللَّهِ مَن آمَنَ بِهِ
"And do not sit on every path, threatening and turning away from the path of Allah those who believe in Him, seeking to make it crooked." — 7:86
Prophet Shu'ayb is sent to Madyan, a trading people in ancient northwest Arabia (modern Tabuk/Jordan area). His message is unique among the prophets in its focus on economic justice: give honest measure and weight; do not shortchange people; do not "sit at every road" intimidating those who believe. Madyan was a commercial hub, and their sin was commercial: they cheated in weights and measures and used their control of trade routes to bully believers and corrupt seekers of truth. This is the Prophet of Economic Justice — making Shu'ayb's message remarkably modern and permanently relevant. Shu'ayb's famous line: "I want only to reform you as much as I can. My success is only through Allah; in Him I have trusted and to Him I return." This is the most concise and beautiful statement of the reformer's ethos in all of prophetic literature.
Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir calls Shu'ayb "Khaṭīb al-Anbiyā'" — the Orator of the Prophets — for the eloquence and persuasiveness of his speech as recorded in the Qur'an. He emphasizes that Shu'ayb's economic message is not separate from his theological message — it flows from it. True tawhid produces economic honesty: if Allah is watching everything, how can you steal in a transaction?
Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn explains "taqʿudūna bi-kulli ṣirāṭ" — sitting on every road — as the use of trade routes and market power to block or intimidate the faithful. Shu'ayb's people used their economic dominance as a weapon against religious freedom. He sees this as a perennial pattern: wealth and political power used to suppress prophetic truth.
Tafsir As-Sa'di
Sheikh As-Sa'di dwells on Shu'ayb's words: "In-urīdu illā al-iṣlāḥ mā istaṭaʿtu" — I want only to reform, to the extent I am able. He notes the intellectual humility: the prophet does not claim he will fix everything. He will do what he can; the rest is in Allah's hands. This is the correct orientation for any person working for good in society — do your part, trust Allah for the outcome.
✨ Key Lessons
  • ⚖️ Economic justice is a prophetic mission — Islam's concern for honest trade is as old as prophecy itself.
  • 🏪 Cheating in business is not "just a financial matter" — it is a moral and spiritual failure directly condemned by a prophet.
  • 🛤️ Using power to block people from faith (sitting on every road) is one of the most serious communal sins.
  • 🙏 Shu'ayb's reformer's motto — "I will do what I can, my success is only with Allah" — is the template for every activist, scholar, and leader in Islam.
  • 🌍 Madyan's sin shows that economic corruption inevitably leads to religious corruption — the two cannot be separated in a healthy society.

📖 Juz 8 — Overarching Themes
A panoramic view of the spiritual and ethical landscape of this Juz
🕌
Pure Tawhid
Every prophet's core message is identical: worship Allah alone. The Juz hammers this truth from every angle.
⚖️
Divine Justice
From the scales of the Day of Judgment to prophetic punishment narratives — justice is a non-negotiable divine attribute.
📜
Prophetic Continuity
Nuh, Hud, Salih, Lut, Shu'ayb — one message, different communities, same pattern of call and response.
💡
Faith as Light
Belief is not just intellectual assent — it is a light that transforms the heart and illuminates the path of life.
😈
Iblis's War on Humanity
The story of Adam reveals the primordial enemy's strategy: arrogance, rationalized disobedience, and multi-directional attack on the straight path.
🌧️
Qur'an as Divine Rain
Just as rain revives dead earth, divine guidance revives the dead heart — the Qur'an is described as mercy and life-giving.
🏺
Economic Ethics
From Al-An'am's halal food laws to Shu'ayb's economic justice — Islam's ethics permeate commerce and daily life.
🙏
Repentance & Mercy
Adam's repentance, the A'raf's hope, the three-day warning for Thamud — divine mercy precedes and outlasts divine judgment.
⏳
Urgency of This Life
Faith will not benefit when the signs arrive. The window of meaningful choice is now — this very moment.
