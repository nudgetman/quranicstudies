# Surah Al-Fatiha — Introduction

**Surah:** 1  
**Arabic Name:** سُورَةُ الْفَاتِحَة  
**English Name:** Al-Fatiha — The Opening  
**Classification:** Makkan  
**Verses:** 7  
**Epithet:** *Umm Al-Kitab* (The Mother of the Book)

---

## Background

Al-Fatiha is the gateway to the Qur'an — a perfect, seven-verse prayer that encapsulates the entire message of Islam. It is recited in every unit (*rak'ah*) of every prayer, making it the most repeated passage in all of Islamic worship.

The Prophet ﷺ called it by several names:
- **Umm Al-Kitab** — the Mother of the Book
- **Al-Sab' Al-Mathani** — the Seven Oft-Repeated Verses
- **Al-Qur'an Al-'Aẓīm** — the Great Qur'an

He also said: *"There is no prayer for one who does not recite Al-Fatiha."* (Bukhari & Muslim)

---

## Why "The Opening"?

Al-Fatiha opens the *muṣḥaf* (physical copy of the Qur'an) by order, but it was not the very first revelation. It was among the earliest surahs revealed in Makkah, and scholars consider it the first *complete* surah revealed in its entirety.

It "opens" the Qur'an in the deepest sense — it is the key with which a person unlocks the rest of the Book. Without understanding Al-Fatiha, the full Qur'an remains a closed door.

---

## Structure

Al-Fatiha divides naturally into three parts:

| Verses | Theme |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | Praise and description of Allah |
| 4 | The Day of Judgement — divine sovereignty |
| 5 | The covenant of worship and dependence |
| 6–7 | The supplication for guidance |

---

## The Hadith Qudsi on Al-Fatiha

Allah says (as recorded in Sahih Muslim):

> *"I have divided the prayer between Myself and My servant into two halves, and My servant shall have what he asks for. When the servant says 'All praise is due to Allah, Lord of all the worlds' — I say: My servant has praised Me. When he says 'The Most Gracious, the Most Merciful' — I say: My servant has extolled Me. When he says 'Master of the Day of Judgement' — I say: My servant has glorified Me. When he says 'You alone we worship and You alone we ask for help' — I say: This is between Me and My servant, and My servant shall have what he asks for. When he says 'Guide us to the straight path…' — I say: This is for My servant, and My servant shall have what he asks for."*

This hadith reveals that Al-Fatiha is a divine conversation — not a monologue. Every verse receives a personal divine response.
