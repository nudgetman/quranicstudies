# Surah Al-Baqarah — Introduction

**Surah:** 2  
**Arabic Name:** سُورَةُ الْبَقَرَة  
**English Name:** Al-Baqarah — The Cow  
**Classification:** Madinan  
**Verses:** 286  
**Distinction:** The longest surah in the Qur'an

---

## Background

Al-Baqarah is the longest surah in the Qur'an, revealed mostly in Madinah after the Hijra (the Prophet's migration from Makkah). It was revealed over a period of approximately ten years and represents the most comprehensive legislative and theological document in the Qur'an.

The Prophet ﷺ said about it:

> *"Recite Al-Baqarah, for taking to it is a blessing and leaving it is a cause of grief, and the magicians cannot confront it."* (Muslim)

> *"The Qur'an will come on the Day of Resurrection and will intercede for its companions. Al-Baqarah and Āl 'Imrān will come like two clouds or two shades or two flocks of birds in ranks, pleading for those who recited them."* (Muslim)

---

## Why Is It Called "The Cow"?

The surah is named after the incident of the slaughtered cow narrated in verses 67–74 — when Banī Isrā'īl were commanded to sacrifice a cow to reveal the murderer of a slain man. The story is not the central theme of the surah but was chosen as its identifying name due to the striking nature of the incident and its profound lessons.

---

## Key Content in Juz 1 (Verses 1–141)

| Passage | Topic |
|---|---|
| 1–5 | The three categories of humanity — believers, disbelievers, hypocrites |
| 21–29 | Universal call to worship; the Qur'anic challenge |
| 30–39 | Creation of Adam and the role of humanity as khalifah |
| 40–101 | Extended address to Banī Isrā'īl — favours and failures |
| 102–112 | Harūt and Mārūt; magic forbidden |
| 113–123 | Sectarianism rebuked; Allah's universal sovereignty |
| 124–135 | Ibrāhīm, the Ka'bah, and the foundations of Islam |
| 136–141 | Universal prophetic faith; *ṣibghat Allāh* |

---

## The Greatest Verse

Al-Baqarah contains **Āyat al-Kursī** (2:255) — the Throne Verse — which the Prophet ﷺ called the greatest verse in the Qur'an:

> *"Whoever recites it after every obligatory prayer, nothing prevents him from entering Paradise except death."*

This verse falls in Juz 3, but its context begins in the foundations laid in Juz 1.
