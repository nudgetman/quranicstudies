# Al-Fatiha 4–5 · Master of the Day & Sole Worship

**Surah:** Al-Fatiha (1) · **Verses:** 4–5  
**Theme:** Divine sovereignty on the Day of Judgement; the covenant of exclusive worship

---

## Arabic Text

> مَالِكِ يَوْمِ الدِّينِ ﴿٤﴾ إِيَّاكَ نَعْبُدُ وَإِيَّاكَ نَسْتَعِينُ ﴿٥﴾

---

## Translation

> *Master of the Day of Judgement. (4) You alone we worship, and You alone we ask for help. (5)*

---

## Explanation

**Māliki yawm id-dīn** — the word *mālik* (master/owner) emphasises that on the Day of Judgement, all apparent authority collapses. Kings, presidents, wealth, influence — all gone. Only Allah's ownership of that Day remains absolute. *Yawm ad-dīn* (the Day of Recompense) is the day when every deed, every hidden intention, every whispered word is repaid in full justice.

Verse 5 is the pivot of the surah — and indeed of the entire Qur'an. The phrasing **Iyyāka** (You alone) places the object **before** the verb in Arabic — a powerful grammatical construction expressing exclusivity. We are not simply saying "we worship" — we are saying "to YOU and none other do we direct our worship."

This single verse defines the core of Islamic monotheism: *tawhīd al-'ibādah* — the Oneness of Allah in worship.

The shift from third-person ("Lord of the worlds") to second-person ("You alone") in verse 5 is theologically profound. Having praised Allah in the first verses, the worshipper now turns **directly** to Him — a spiritual intimacy achieved through praise. Prayer is a conversation, not a monologue.

---

## Scholar Callouts

### 📖 Tafsir Ibn Kathir
Ibn Kathir notes the powerful grammatical emphasis of placing *iyyāka* first: it restricts worship and help-seeking exclusively to Allah, eliminating every form of shirk. He records the Hadith Qudsi regarding this surah:

> *"When My servant says 'iyyāka na'budu wa iyyāka nasta'in' — this is between Me and My servant, and My servant shall have what he asks for."*

This verse is the very heart of the covenant between the servant and his Lord.

### 📖 Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
Al-Jalalayn explains that *nasta'in* (we seek help) encompasses seeking Allah's aid in **all matters** — worship, livelihood, knowledge, and every affair of life. No human being is self-sufficient; ultimate dependency belongs only to Allah.

### 📖 Tafsir As-Sa'di
Shaykh As-Sa'di writes that this verse unites the two greatest obligations on the servant: *'ibādah* (worship) and *tawakkul* (reliance on Allah). Worship is the goal; seeking His help is the means to achieve that goal. Together they represent the complete religion — establishing the right of Allah and acknowledging our own utter need.

---

## ✨ Key Lessons

- ⚖️ The Day of Judgement is real — every action we take today will be accounted for.
- 🕌 True worship means directing all devotion, hope, fear, love, and obedience to Allah alone.
- 🤲 *Nasta'in* — never forget that you need Allah's help in everything, even in worshipping Him.
- 🔄 The shift from third to second person teaches us: praising Allah draws you closer to Him in intimate conversation.
- 🚫 Shirk — giving anyone Allah's rights of worship — is the only unforgivable sin (if died upon). This verse guards us from it.
