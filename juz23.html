Sūrah 36
يس
Yā-Sīn · "The Heart of the Qur'ān"
83 Verses Meccan Juz 23: vv. 22–83

Yā-Sīn is often called "the heart of the Qur'ān." It addresses the certainty of resurrection, the signs of Allāh in the natural world, and the fate of those who reject the messengers. The Prophet ﷺ said: "Everything has a heart, and the heart of the Qur'ān is Yā-Sīn." (Tirmidhī)

36:22–27 The Believing Man from the Far End of the City
وَمَا لِيَ لَا أَعْبُدُ الَّذِي فَطَرَنِي وَإِلَيْهِ تُرْجَعُونَ ﴿٢٢﴾ أَأَتَّخِذُ مِن دُونِهِ آلِهَةً إِن يُرِدْنِ الرَّحْمَٰنُ بِضُرٍّ لَّا تُغْنِ عَنِّي شَفَاعَتُهُمْ شَيْئًا وَلَا يُنقِذُونِ ﴿٢٣﴾ إِنِّي إِذًا لَّفِي ضَلَالٍ مُّبِينٍ ﴿٢٤﴾ إِنِّي آمَنتُ بِرَبِّكُمْ فَاسْمَعُونِ ﴿٢٥﴾ قِيلَ ادْخُلِ الْجَنَّةَ ۖ قَالَ يَا لَيْتَ قَوْمِي يَعْلَمُونَ ﴿٢٦﴾ بِمَا غَفَرَ لِي رَبِّي وَجَعَلَنِي مِنَ الْمُكْرَمِينَ ﴿٢٧﴾
"And why should I not worship He who created me and to Whom you will be returned? Should I take other than Him as gods? If the Most Merciful intends harm for me, their intercession will not avail me at all, nor can they save me. Indeed, I would then be in manifest error. Indeed, I have believed in your Lord, so listen to me." It was said: "Enter Paradise." He said: "Oh, I wish my people could know of how my Lord has forgiven me and placed me among the honoured." (36:22–27)
This passage introduces one of the Qur'ān's most moving supporting characters — an unnamed man who rushed from the far side of the city when he heard that three messengers had been sent. While his people were hostile to the prophets, this lone believer stood up and made his case with breathtaking logic: why would I worship idols that cannot intercede for me or save me from Allāh's will? He declared his faith publicly — and was killed for it. Yet instead of bitterness, his first thought upon entering Paradise was for his people. "I wish they could know!" — not "I wish they could suffer" — reveals the purest form of faith: love without resentment.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr identifies this man as Ḥabīb al-Najjār, a carpenter or dyer who believed in the messengers of 'Īsā (Jesus). He came running (sa'ā), which implies great urgency and sincerity. His question "What is wrong with me that I should not worship He who created me?" is a call to rational reflection — the creation itself points to the Creator. Ibn Kathīr notes that his concern for his people after death demonstrates the pinnacle of selfless faith.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn explains his words as a syllogism: worshipping anything other than the One who created me is self-evidently irrational. His proclamation "Listen to me!" is directed at his people as a final sincere plea. His entry into Paradise was immediate — Allāh rewarded him for his bold testimony. His wish for his people to know — not a desire for praise, but genuine concern for their salvation.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī observes that this man's argument follows the natural disposition (fiṭrah): the One who brought you into existence has the sole right to your worship. False gods offer nothing — no benefit, no protection, no intercession when it truly matters. His final words from Paradise are described as an expression of true brotherhood in faith — he had no grudge, only love and hope for those who had harmed him.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • 🧠 Faith grounded in logic — the existence of a Creator demands worship of only that Creator.
  • 🕊️ Idols and false intercessors are powerless when Allāh's decree comes.
  • ❤️ Genuine belief produces love for others, even those who oppose you.
  • ⚡ Courage to publicly declare the truth, even at the cost of your life, is rewarded immediately by Allāh.
  • 🌿 Death for a believer is not an end — it is an entrance into honour.
36:33–44 Signs of Allāh in the Natural World
وَآيَةٌ لَّهُمُ الْأَرْضُ الْمَيْتَةُ أَحْيَيْنَاهَا وَأَخْرَجْنَا مِنْهَا حَبًّا فَمِنْهُ يَأْكُلُونَ ﴿٣٣﴾ وَجَعَلْنَا فِيهَا جَنَّاتٍ مِّن نَّخِيلٍ وَأَعْنَابٍ وَفَجَّرْنَا فِيهَا مِنَ الْعُيُونِ ﴿٣٤﴾ وَآيَةٌ لَّهُمُ اللَّيْلُ نَسْلَخُ مِنْهُ النَّهَارَ فَإِذَا هُم مُّظْلِمُونَ ﴿٣٧﴾ وَالشَّمْسُ تَجْرِي لِمُسْتَقَرٍّ لَّهَا ۚ ذَٰلِكَ تَقْدِيرُ الْعَزِيزِ الْعَلِيمِ ﴿٣٨﴾
"And a sign for them is the dead earth. We gave it life and brought forth from it grain, and from it they eat. And We placed therein gardens of palm trees and grapevines and caused to burst forth therefrom some springs... And a sign for them is the night. We remove from it the day, so they are left in darkness. And the sun runs on its course to a stopping point. That is the decree of the Exalted in Might, the Knowing." (36:33–38)
After the story of the city, Allāh presents three magnificent proofs of His power: the dead earth revived by rain, the alternation of night and day, and the orbits of the sun and moon. These are not just beautiful descriptions of nature — they are arguments for resurrection. If Allāh can bring dead soil back to life with seeds and rain, why is it difficult to believe He can bring dead people back to life on Judgment Day? Every sunrise and sunset is a reminder that Allāh governs time itself.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr draws a direct parallel: just as rain revives dead earth, the Trumpet of Resurrection will revive dead bodies. He notes that the Qur'ān consistently uses natural phenomena as rational arguments for the impossible-seeming idea of resurrection. "The sun runs to a fixed resting place" — Ibn Kathīr cites that this refers to a point under Allāh's throne where the sun prostrates each night before being permitted to rise again.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn explains "We remove day from night like skin being peeled" — a vivid metaphor for how darkness envelops the world as light withdraws. The precise mathematical orbits of the sun and moon — each swimming in its designated lane — reflect the perfect ordering of a Master Planner. Nothing in the cosmos is accidental.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī emphasises that these signs are presented for those who reflect. Allāh does not simply assert His power — He points to the observable world. The revival of the earth is experienced by every farmer, every traveller in the desert. Denying resurrection while accepting that dead earth comes back to life is an inconsistency that Allāh gently exposes.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • 🌱 The natural world is a continuous proof of Allāh's existence and power.
  • ☀️ The precise movement of celestial bodies is not chance — it is divine engineering.
  • 💧 Rain reviving dead earth is an annual proof of the coming Resurrection.
  • 🔭 Observation of creation is an act of worship when done with reflection.
  • 🌙 Every night and day is a mercy — Allāh arranged cycles for rest and productivity.
36:51–65 The Day of Resurrection — The Trumpet Blast
وَنُفِخَ فِي الصُّورِ فَإِذَا هُم مِّنَ الْأَجْدَاثِ إِلَىٰ رَبِّهِمْ يَنسِلُونَ ﴿٥١﴾ قَالُوا يَا وَيْلَنَا مَن بَعَثَنَا مِن مَّرْقَدِنَا ۜ هَٰذَا مَا وَعَدَ الرَّحْمَٰنُ وَصَدَقَ الْمُرْسَلُونَ ﴿٥٢﴾ الْيَوْمَ نَخْتِمُ عَلَىٰ أَفْوَاهِهِمْ وَتُكَلِّمُنَا أَيْدِيهِمْ وَتَشْهَدُ أَرْجُلُهُم بِمَا كَانُوا يَكْسِبُونَ ﴿٦٥﴾
"And the Trumpet will be blown, and at once they will be rushing from the graves to their Lord. They will say: 'Woe to us! Who has raised us up from our sleeping place?' [They will be told:] 'This is what the Most Merciful promised, and the messengers spoke the truth.'... That Day, We will seal over their mouths, and their hands will speak to Us, and their feet will testify about what they used to earn." (36:51–52, 65)
This is one of the most vivid depictions of Resurrection in the Qur'ān. The dead, in their graves, will feel as if they had been napping — so light will the barzakh feel compared to what comes. They will stagger out, bewildered. Then Allāh will silence their tongues — no more excuses, no more denials — and their own hands and feet will testify against them. The body becomes a witness against its owner. Every step taken in disobedience, every hand raised in harm — all recorded in the limbs themselves.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr notes the dramatic shift: people who mocked resurrection will suddenly be living it. Their own exclamation — "Who woke us from our beds?" — confirms that the grave was a place of sleep or torment depending on one's deeds. The believers among them will answer: "This is what the Merciful promised." The sealing of the mouth is a mercy in the sense that denial becomes impossible — truth emerges from the body itself.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn explains that the rushing from graves (yansilūn) suggests hasty movement, overwhelmed and confused. The phrase "sleeping place" used by the disbelievers implies they expected the sleep of death to be permanent. Their own testimony against themselves is the ultimate justice — no external witnesses needed when the limbs themselves testify.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī highlights that Allāh is called "Raḥmān — the Most Merciful" even in the context of Judgment Day — because even the arrangement of Resurrection is an act of mercy: every soul receives perfect justice, not approximation. The speaking of hands and feet is literal, created speech in the limbs by Allāh's power on that Day.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • 📯 Resurrection is certain — even those who denied it will confirm it with their own words.
  • 🤐 On that Day, excuses end — the body itself is the evidence.
  • 👁️ Every action is being recorded — not just in books, but in our own limbs.
  • ⚖️ Divine justice is perfect — no soul bears another's burden, no crime goes unaccounted.
  • 🌅 The messengers were truthful — what they warned about will come exactly as promised.
36:77–83 The Origin of Creation — "Be, and It Is"
أَوَلَمْ يَرَ الْإِنسَانُ أَنَّا خَلَقْنَاهُ مِن نُّطْفَةٍ فَإِذَا هُوَ خَصِيمٌ مُّبِينٌ ﴿٧٧﴾ وَضَرَبَ لَنَا مَثَلًا وَنَسِيَ خَلْقَهُ ۚ قَالَ مَن يُحْيِي الْعِظَامَ وَهِيَ رَمِيمٌ ﴿٧٨﴾ قُلْ يُحْيِيهَا الَّذِي أَنشَأَهَا أَوَّلَ مَرَّةٍ ۖ وَهُوَ بِكُلِّ خَلْقٍ عَلِيمٌ ﴿٧٩﴾ إِنَّمَا أَمْرُهُ إِذَا أَرَادَ شَيْئًا أَن يَقُولَ لَهُ كُن فَيَكُونُ ﴿٨٢﴾ فَسُبْحَانَ الَّذِي بِيَدِهِ مَلَكُوتُ كُلِّ شَيْءٍ وَإِلَيْهِ تُرْجَعُونَ ﴿٨٣﴾
"Does man not consider that We created him from a drop of sperm — yet he is an open adversary? And he presents an argument and forgets his own creation. He says: 'Who will give life to bones when they are disintegrated?' Say: 'He will give them life who produced them the first time, and He is of all creation Knowing.'... His command is only when He intends a thing that He says to it: 'Be,' and it is. So exalted is He in whose hand is the dominion of all things, and to Him you will be returned." (36:77–79, 82–83)
Sūrah Yā-Sīn closes with the most direct refutation of disbelief in resurrection. The disbeliever waves a crumbling bone and says: who can bring this back? The answer is devastating in its simplicity — the same One who made it from nothing the first time. If you accept that you were created from a microscopic drop of fluid, how is it harder to believe that Allāh can reassemble bones? The surah ends on "Kun fa-yakūn" — Be, and it is — the most complete statement of divine omnipotence in the Qur'ān.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr narrates that this passage was revealed about a Qurayshi man (Ubayy ibn Khalaf) who brought a crumbling bone to the Prophet ﷺ and crumbled it in his hands mockingly, saying "who will resurrect this?" The Qur'ān's response is that creation from nothing is a greater miracle than recreation — so denying the latter while accepting the former is illogical. "He is of all creation Knowing" — He knows every atom of every bone.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn explains "forgets his own creation" as the core of human ingratitude — we accept our own origin from humble beginnings without questioning it, yet question Allāh's ability to recreate. The word "reem" (disintegrated) suggests utter powder — not merely broken, but dissolved — yet even this is no obstacle for Allāh.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī notes that Kun fa-yakūn is not about the speed of creation — it's about the nature of Allāh's will. His intention and its fulfilment are one. There is no gap, no effort, no intermediary between His will and reality. The verse ends by reminding that everything returns to Him — the universe is not running away from Allāh, it is running toward its meeting with Him.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • 🧬 We were created from something tiny — denying resurrection is forgetting our own origin.
  • 💡 Creation from nothing is greater than recreation — the first time is the harder argument to deny.
  • ⚡ Allāh's command needs no effort — He wills, and it is done.
  • 🌍 Everything in existence belongs to Allāh and returns to Him — nothing is ours permanently.
  • 🙏 "Subḥān" — glory be to Allāh — is the natural response when you truly understand His power.

Sūrah 37
الصَّافَّات
Aṣ-Ṣāffāt · "Those Who Range in Ranks"
182 Verses Meccan Complete in Juz 23

Named after the opening oath by the angels arrayed in ranks, this surah presents the divine court of Allāh and then tells the stories of Nūḥ, Ibrāhīm, Ismā'īl, Mūsā, Hārūn, Ilyās, Lūṭ, and Yūnus — a gallery of prophets who trusted Allāh and were saved. It closes by refuting those who claim Allāh has daughters or companions.

37:1–10 The Oath of the Angelic Ranks
وَالصَّافَّاتِ صَفًّا ﴿١﴾ فَالزَّاجِرَاتِ زَجْرًا ﴿٢﴾ فَالتَّالِيَاتِ ذِكْرًا ﴿٣﴾ إِنَّ إِلَٰهَكُمْ لَوَاحِدٌ ﴿٤﴾ رَّبُّ السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضِ وَمَا بَيْنَهُمَا وَرَبُّ الْمَشَارِقِ ﴿٥﴾ وَحَفِظْنَا السَّمَاءَ الدُّنْيَا مِن كُلِّ شَيْطَانٍ مَّارِدٍ ﴿٧﴾
"By those [angels] ranged in rows, and those who drive [the clouds], and those who recite the reminder — indeed, your God is One: Lord of the heavens and the earth and whatever is between them, and Lord of the sunrises... And We have guarded the nearest heaven against every rebellious devil." (37:1–7)
Allāh opens with a triple oath by the angels — those who stand in perfect rows of worship, those who repel evil, and those who recite revelation. The result of this oath is the most important truth: your God is ONE. The majesty of the angelic host, arrayed in discipline and devotion, is itself a proof of divine order. The cosmos is not chaos — it is governed by a Single sovereign whose angels protect even the sky from satanic intrusion.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr explains that the three descriptions refer to angels: (1) those who stand in ranks before Allāh in perfect worship, (2) those who drive away evil, including rainfall clouds and satanic whispers, (3) those who carry divine revelation to prophets. The oath leads to the core declaration of Tawḥīd — the singleness of Allāh — as if the entire angelic universe is bearing witness to this truth.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn notes that Allāh guards the lower heaven (the nearest sky) with shooting stars that strike devils attempting to eavesdrop on the angelic assembly. This explains why fortune-telling and contact with jinn for "unseen knowledge" is forbidden — the channels are blocked, and any fragment of truth that slips through is mixed with many lies.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī highlights that Allāh swears by His own creation (the angels) as a rhetorical elevation — to say: look at what I have created in perfect order and rank, and then know that I am One. The disciplined worship of angels is a model for human worship: orderly, devoted, free from distraction.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • 👼 The angels worship Allāh in perfect rows — our congregational prayer mirrors this heavenly discipline.
  • ☝️ The central truth of Tawḥīd is the conclusion of the greatest oath in creation.
  • 🌌 Allāh actively guards His creation — the heavens are not left unprotected.
  • 🚫 Devil's access to divine knowledge is blocked — fortune-telling has no real basis.
  • 🕌 Orderly, structured worship (ṣalāh in rows) is beloved to Allāh.
37:19–49 The Day of Judgment — Hell, Paradise, and the Blessed Companions
فَإِنَّمَا هِيَ زَجْرَةٌ وَاحِدَةٌ فَإِذَا هُمْ يَنظُرُونَ ﴿١٩﴾ وَقَالُوا يَا وَيْلَنَا هَٰذَا يَوْمُ الدِّينِ ﴿٢٠﴾ إِنَّ الْمُتَّقِينَ فِي جَنَّاتٍ وَنَعِيمٍ ﴿٤١﴾ فَاكِهِينَ بِمَا آتَاهُمْ رَبُّهُمْ ۚ وَوَقَاهُمْ رَبُّهُمْ عَذَابَ الْجَحِيمِ ﴿٤٣﴾ وَحُورٌ عِينٌ ﴿٤٨﴾ كَأَنَّهُنَّ بَيْضٌ مَّكْنُونٌ ﴿٤٩﴾
"It will be only a single blast, and at once they will be looking on. They will say: 'Woe to us! This is the Day of Recompense.'... Indeed, the righteous will be in gardens and pleasure, delighting in what their Lord has given them, and their Lord will have protected them from the torment of Hellfire..." (37:19–20, 41–43)
One single shout — and history is over. All the centuries of life, conflict, and denial dissolve in a moment. Then the sorting begins: the righteous to gardens of delight, the wrongdoers to face what they dismissed. The contrast is sharp and intentional. The people of Paradise are described with the word "fākihīn" — rejoicing, delighting — suggesting not just safety from harm but active, vibrant joy. They are not merely rescued; they are celebrated.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr explains that the "single blast" is the second blowing of the Trumpet (the first causes death, the second causes resurrection). The suddenness — "at once they are looking" — illustrates that Resurrection is not a gradual process. The description of the people of Paradise as "fākihīn" — those who take delight — indicates that their enjoyment is self-generated from within; they are not passive recipients but active participants in their bliss.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn notes the conversation between the people of Paradise and the people of Hell (vv. 50–68) as a window into the afterlife's social reality. Believers will ask about their old acquaintances, and when they look down, they will see them in the midst of torment — a sobering reminder of the divergent ends that await people who once shared the same world.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī draws attention to "their Lord protected them" — the protection is attributed to Allāh Himself, not to angels or their own deeds alone. This is a statement of divine mercy: the believer's entry into Paradise is ultimately a gift from Allāh. Their deeds were the means, but Allāh's grace is the door.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • ⚡ The Day of Judgment comes in an instant — preparation must be now, not later.
  • 🌿 Paradise is not just the absence of punishment — it is active, joyful delight.
  • 🤲 Our entry into mercy is ultimately from Allāh's grace, not our deeds alone.
  • 👥 The choices we make define our eternal company — choose your path in this life carefully.
  • 💔 Seeing those you knew in torment will be among the great sorrows of believers — warn those around you now.
37:83–113 The Story of Ibrāhīm — The Friend of Allāh and the Great Sacrifice
وَإِنَّ مِن شِيعَتِهِ لَإِبْرَاهِيمَ ﴿٨٣﴾ إِذْ جَاءَ رَبَّهُ بِقَلْبٍ سَلِيمٍ ﴿٨٤﴾ فَلَمَّا بَلَغَ مَعَهُ السَّعْيَ قَالَ يَا بُنَيَّ إِنِّي أَرَىٰ فِي الْمَنَامِ أَنِّي أَذْبَحُكَ فَانظُرْ مَاذَا تَرَىٰ ﴿١٠٢﴾ قَالَ يَا أَبَتِ افْعَلْ مَا تُؤْمَرُ ۖ سَتَجِدُنِي إِن شَاءَ اللَّهُ مِنَ الصَّابِرِينَ ﴿١٠٢﴾ فَلَمَّا أَسْلَمَا وَتَلَّهُ لِلْجَبِينِ ﴿١٠٣﴾ وَنَادَيْنَاهُ أَن يَا إِبْرَاهِيمُ ﴿١٠٤﴾ قَدْ صَدَّقْتَ الرُّؤْيَا ﴿١٠٥﴾
"And indeed, among his followers was Abraham, when he came to his Lord with a pure heart... When he reached the age of working with him, he said: 'O my son, indeed I have seen in a dream that I must sacrifice you, so see what you think.' He said: 'O my father, do as you are commanded. You will find me, if Allāh wills, of the steadfast.' And when they had both submitted and he put him down upon his forehead, We called to him: 'O Abraham! You have fulfilled the vision.'" (37:83–84, 102–105)
This is one of the most profound moments in prophetic history. Ibrāhīm, tested his entire life — thrown into fire, expelled from his home, commanded to leave his wife and infant in a barren valley — now faces the ultimate trial: sacrificing his son. What makes this extraordinary is the son's response. Ismā'īl (the majority view) did not flee or resist — he accepted with complete surrender. Both father and son submitted (aslama) — the word echoes the very meaning of Islām. Allāh did not need the sacrifice. He needed to see the heart.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr discusses the identity of the son at length. The stronger opinion among scholars is that it was Ismā'īl, based on context (this surah mentions Ismā'īl as a second gift after Isḥāq in v. 112, implying the son of sacrifice was Ismā'īl). The phrase "he put him face down upon his forehead" shows the boy actively cooperating — he laid himself down. The ransom of a great ram was sent by Allāh and accepted in his place.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn explains "You have fulfilled the vision" — the act of submission itself was the fulfilment. Allāh counts our sincere intentions as completed deeds when our hearts are truly resolved. The ram that appeared was already destined from pre-eternity to be this specific ransom — nothing was improvised.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī reflects on "qalb salīm — a pure heart" as the foundation of Ibrāhīm's entire life. Every trial he passed was rooted in this: a heart free of attachment to anything other than Allāh. Our greatest obstacle to obedience is love of the worldly — Ibrāhīm's story shows what is possible when the heart is cleansed of that attachment. This is why Eid al-Aḍḥā commemorates not just a sacrifice, but a surrender.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • ❤️ A "pure heart" (qalb salīm) is the highest human achievement — free from attachment to other than Allāh.
  • 🤝 True Islām is submission — not just of the tongue but of the heart and body.
  • 👨‍👦 When a father raises his child in faith, the child becomes his partner in obedience to Allāh.
  • 🐏 Eid al-Aḍḥā is a celebration of surrender — the sacrifice is symbolic of giving up what we love most.
  • ✅ Allāh rewards the intention — sincere resolve is counted as completed action.
37:139–148 Yūnus — The Prophet in the Belly of the Whale
وَإِنَّ يُونُسَ لَمِنَ الْمُرْسَلِينَ ﴿١٣٩﴾ إِذْ أَبَقَ إِلَى الْفُلْكِ الْمَشْحُونِ ﴿١٤٠﴾ فَسَاهَمَ فَكَانَ مِنَ الْمُدْحَضِينَ ﴿١٤١﴾ فَالْتَقَمَهُ الْحُوتُ وَهُوَ مُلِيمٌ ﴿١٤٢﴾ فَلَوْلَا أَنَّهُ كَانَ مِنَ الْمُسَبِّحِينَ ﴿١٤٣﴾ لَلَبِثَ فِي بَطْنِهِ إِلَىٰ يَوْمِ يُبْعَثُونَ ﴿١٤٤﴾ فَنَبَذْنَاهُ بِالْعَرَاءِ وَهُوَ سَقِيمٌ ﴿١٤٥﴾ وَأَنبَتْنَا عَلَيْهِ شَجَرَةً مِّن يَقْطِينٍ ﴿١٤٦﴾
"And indeed, Yūnus was among the messengers, when he ran away to the laden ship. And he drew lots, and was among the losers. And the fish swallowed him while he was blameworthy. And had he not been among those who exalted Allāh, he would have remained inside its belly until the Day they are resurrected. But We threw him onto the open shore, while he was ill, and We caused a gourd plant to grow over him." (37:139–146)
Yūnus left his people without divine permission, frustrated that they had not responded to his call. The whale became his prison — and his classroom. In three layers of darkness (the night, the depths of the sea, and the belly of the whale), he returned to Allāh with the famous supplication: "There is no deity except You; exalted are You. Indeed, I have been of the wrongdoers." His rescue is attributed directly to his habit of glorifying Allāh — "had he not been among those who exalt Allāh." This is the Qur'ān telling us: your dhikr before the crisis is what saves you in the crisis.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr explains "he fled like a slave fleeing his master" — not in physical cowardice but in a prophet's premature departure without permission. The lot-casting on the ship (a practice of that time to determine who would be thrown overboard in dangerous seas) fell on him three times, pointing to divine arrangement. The whale's belly was not merely a prison but a place of repentance — and his tasbīḥ before the test was what made his rescue possible.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn notes that the gourd plant (yaqṭīn, a type of gourd or pumpkin) was given as a mercy — shade, food, and medicine for his illness. Nothing in Allāh's care is improvised. The detail that he emerged ill but was fed and sheltered shows that rescue from one difficulty does not mean immediate comfort — but Allāh's care is continuous.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī draws the powerful lesson: "had he not been of those who exalt Allāh" — the practice of dhikr accumulated before the crisis is what released him. This is why the Prophet ﷺ encouraged remembering Allāh in times of ease, for the angels bear witness in times of hardship. Your connection to Allāh in ordinary moments is your lifeline in extraordinary ones.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • 🐋 No darkness is too deep for Allāh's light — not three layers of dark, not the belly of a whale.
  • 📿 Dhikr in times of ease is your rescue rope in times of hardship — invest in it now.
  • 🙏 Genuine repentance — acknowledging your own wrongdoing — is the key that opens the door of mercy.
  • 🌿 After every rescue, Allāh tends to you — His care does not stop at survival.
  • ⚠️ Even prophets are corrected — impatience and acting without permission are lessons for all of us.

Sūrah 38
ص
Ṣād · The Letter Ṣ
88 Verses Meccan Complete in Juz 23 Contains Sajdah (v. 24)

Named after its opening mysterious letter, Ṣād features the stories of Dāwūd, Sulaymān, and Ayyūb — three kings and prophets tested in very different ways. It concludes with the creation of Adam and Iblīs's refusal to prostrate, setting the stage for the human-devil drama of history. The surah contains a verse of prostration (sajdah tilāwah) at verse 24.

38:1–11 The Arrogance of the Disbelievers — "This Is Astonishing"
ص ۚ وَالْقُرْآنِ ذِي الذِّكْرِ ﴿١﴾ بَلِ الَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا فِي عِزَّةٍ وَشِقَاقٍ ﴿٢﴾ وَعَجِبُوا أَن جَاءَهُم مُّنذِرٌ مِّنْهُمْ ۖ وَقَالَ الْكَافِرُونَ هَٰذَا سَاحِرٌ كَذَّابٌ ﴿٤﴾ أَجَعَلَ الْآلِهَةَ إِلَٰهًا وَاحِدًا ۖ إِنَّ هَٰذَا لَشَيْءٌ عُجَابٌ ﴿٥﴾
"Ṣ. By the Qur'ān containing reminder. But those who disbelieve are in false pride and dissension. They were astonished that a warner came to them from among themselves. And the disbelievers said: 'This is a magician and a liar. Has he made the gods into one God? Indeed, this is an astonishing thing!'" (38:1–5)
The Meccan leaders were not confused by Tawḥīd — they were offended by it. "Has he made the gods into ONE?" — as if reducing their pantheon was an insult to their identity and social order. The concept of one God was not intellectually difficult for them; it was socially and economically disruptive. Their rejection came from pride ('izzah) — that same arrogance that drives humans to reject truth when accepting it would cost them status.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr notes that the Quraysh aristocrats were furious that Muḥammad ﷺ — a member of their own community — would claim prophethood and call them to abandon their ancestral religion. Their mockery of "one god only" exposes their true motive: social prestige, trade alliances linked to the Ka'bah's idols, and tribal ego. The oath "By the Qur'ān of reminder" emphasises that this very Book they mock is the proof against them.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn explains "false pride and dissension" (fī 'izzatin wa-shiqāq) — they were not open to dialogue; they had already decided. Shiqāq means splitting away from truth, choosing opposition as an identity. This is a spiritual disease: when rejecting truth becomes a matter of ego rather than evidence.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī observes that their astonishment at a human messenger is ironic — who else would Allāh send to humans but a human? An angel would be too alien, too inaccessible. The Prophet's humanity was a mercy, not a weakness. Their accusation of magic and lying was the oldest deflection: when you cannot refute the message, attack the messenger.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • 🪞 Pride is the root of rejection — truth is often refused not because it's unclear but because it's costly to the ego.
  • 🤝 Allāh sends human messengers as a mercy — they are relatable, approachable models.
  • ⚔️ Attacking the messenger instead of engaging the message is a sign of intellectual dishonesty.
  • 📣 Social and economic interests often drive religious rejection more than theological disagreement.
  • 💎 The Qur'ān is described as "dhī al-dhikr" — full of reminder — it contains everything needed to guide.
38:21–26 Dāwūd and the Two Disputants — The Verse of Prostration ۩
وَهَلْ أَتَاكَ نَبَأُ الْخَصْمِ إِذْ تَسَوَّرُوا الْمِحْرَابَ ﴿٢١﴾ إِذْ دَخَلُوا عَلَىٰ دَاوُودَ فَفَزِعَ مِنْهُمْ ۖ قَالُوا لَا تَخَفْ ۖ خَصْمَانِ بَغَىٰ بَعْضُنَا عَلَىٰ بَعْضٍ فَاحْكُم بَيْنَنَا بِالْحَقِّ ﴿٢٢﴾ وَظَنَّ دَاوُودُ أَنَّمَا فَتَنَّاهُ فَاسْتَغْفَرَ رَبَّهُ وَخَرَّ رَاكِعًا وَأَنَابَ ۩ ﴿٢٤﴾ فَغَفَرْنَا لَهُ ذَٰلِكَ ۖ وَإِنَّ لَهُ عِندَنَا لَزُلْفَىٰ وَحُسْنَ مَآبٍ ﴿٢٥﴾
"And has there come to you the news of the adversaries when they climbed over the wall of his prayer chamber? When they entered upon David and he was alarmed by them, they said: 'Fear not. We are two adversaries, one of whom has wronged the other, so judge between us in truth.'... And David became certain that We had tried him, so he sought forgiveness of his Lord, and fell down bowing [in prostration] ۩ and turned in repentance. So We forgave him that; and indeed, he has nearness to Us and a good place of return." (38:21–22, 24–25)
Two strangers scaled the wall of Dāwūd's prayer chamber — an unusual, almost theatrical arrival. They presented a case: a man with 99 ewes had demanded the one ewe of his companion. Dāwūd ruled immediately against the man of 99. Then he realised — this was a test. The angels had presented a parable. Dāwūd fell in prostration, weeping. Allāh forgave him instantly. The prostration in verse 24 is the Sajdah Tilāwah — when reciting or hearing this verse, it is sunnah to perform a prostration to Allāh.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr reports various explanations of what Dāwūd's trial was. The dominant scholarly view is that he was tested in a matter related to judging between people, or in a desire he harboured — the Qur'ān does not specify the exact nature of his lapse, and scholars caution against elaborating beyond what Allāh revealed. What is clear is that Dāwūd recognised the trial immediately, made sincere tawbah (repentance), and was completely forgiven. His story is one of the most powerful examples of how swiftly Allāh responds to genuine repentance.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn explains the sajdah in verse 24 — Dāwūd literally "fell down bowing" (kharra rāki'an) — some scholars interpret this as a sajdah (prostration), which is why this verse is a place of prostration for the reciter. His repentance was immediate and physical — his body expressed what his heart felt. The fast, complete forgiveness that followed illustrates Allāh's attribute: He is Ghaffār — the Ever-Forgiving.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī highlights the beautiful result: "nearness to Us and a good place of return." Dāwūd's slip, followed by genuine repentance, actually brought him closer to Allāh than if he had never been tested. This is the paradox of tawbah — sincere repentance can elevate a servant above their pre-sin station. The test was not punishment; it was an elevation in disguise.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • ۩ This verse contains a Sajdah Tilāwah — prostrate to Allāh when you read or hear it.
  • 🔍 Even great prophets were tested — our own trials are a form of divine attention, not abandonment.
  • ⚡ Dāwūd recognised his test immediately and turned back — speed of repentance is itself a sign of nearness to Allāh.
  • 🌸 Sincere tawbah not only wipes sins — it can elevate your station above where you were before.
  • ⚖️ Leaders and judges must guard against bias in all its subtle forms — justice is Allāh's trust.
38:30–40 Sulaymān's Trials — Horses, Wealth, and Gratitude
وَوَهَبْنَا لِدَاوُودَ سُلَيْمَانَ ۚ نِعْمَ الْعَبْدُ ۖ إِنَّهُ أَوَّابٌ ﴿٣٠﴾ وَوَهَبْنَا لِسُلَيْمَانَ الرِّيحَ غُدُوُّهَا شَهْرٌ وَرَوَاحُهَا شَهْرٌ ﴿٣٦﴾ قَالَ رَبِّ اغْفِرْ لِي وَهَبْ لِي مُلْكًا لَّا يَنبَغِي لِأَحَدٍ مِّن بَعْدِي ﴿٣٥﴾
"And We gave to David, Solomon. An excellent servant, indeed he was one repeatedly turning back [to Allāh]... And to Solomon We subjected the wind — its morning journey was a month's worth and its evening journey a month's worth... He said: 'My Lord, forgive me and grant me a kingdom such as will not belong to anyone after me.'" (38:30, 36, 35)
Sulaymān inherited his father's prophethood and was given the most extraordinary kingdom in human history: control of wind, jinn, and the language of birds. Yet the Qur'ān praises him not for his power but for being an "awwāb" — one who perpetually returns to Allāh. His request for an unprecedented kingdom was not arrogance; it was a strategic dedication of power to the service of Allāh's dīn. The test of horses shows his relationship with wealth: when they distracted him from dhikr, he gave them up entirely.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr explains the famous horse episode: Sulaymān was absorbed in reviewing fine horses until the sun set, causing him to miss the 'Aṣr prayer (or its time of dhikr). Recognising his error, he had the horses brought back and sacrificed them as charity to remind himself that no worldly possession — not even his magnificent warhorses — should divert him from Allāh. His unique kingdom request was granted — to this day, no human ruler has commanded wind, jinn, and nature simultaneously.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn notes that the wind travelled a month's journey in a morning — a miracle of divine engineering. The jinn (shayāṭīn) were placed under his command and shackled, their destructive energy channelled into construction and craftsmanship. This illustrates Allāh's absolute sovereignty — even the most rebellious forces are ultimately under His control and can be harnessed for good.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī reflects on the praise "ni'm al-'abd — what an excellent servant" applied to both Dāwūd and Sulaymān. The criterion of excellence is not power or miracles — it is awwāb: constantly returning to Allāh. The greatest of kings are great because they never forgot they were servants. Their power was a trust, not a possession.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • 👑 True excellence is in returning to Allāh (awwāb), not in accumulating power or wealth.
  • ⏰ No occupation — however noble — should delay us from our obligations to Allāh.
  • 🌬️ Allāh's gifts to His servants can be extraordinary — nothing is beyond His ability to grant.
  • 🔑 Wealth and power are trusts, not possessions — using them for Allāh's service is what makes them blessed.
  • 🙏 Even prophets asked Allāh for worldly gifts — doing so with the right intention is not greed.
38:41–44 Ayyūb — The Prophet of Patience
وَاذْكُرْ عَبْدَنَا أَيُّوبَ إِذْ نَادَىٰ رَبَّهُ أَنِّي مَسَّنِيَ الضُّرُّ وَأَنتَ أَرْحَمُ الرَّاحِمِينَ ﴿٤١﴾ فَاسْتَجَبْنَا لَهُ فَكَشَفْنَا مَا بِهِ مِن ضُرٍّ ۖ وَآتَيْنَاهُ أَهْلَهُ وَمِثْلَهُم مَّعَهُمْ رَحْمَةً مِّنَّا وَذِكْرَىٰ لِأُولِي الْأَلْبَابِ ﴿٤٣﴾
"And remember Our servant Job, when he called to his Lord: 'Adversity has touched me, and You are the Most Merciful of the merciful.' So We responded to him and removed what afflicted him of adversity. And We gave back his family and the like of them with them as mercy from Us and a reminder for those of understanding." (38:41, 43)
Ayyūb's supplication is a masterpiece of prophetic du'ā: he does not command, demand, or bargain. He simply states his reality — "Adversity has touched me" — and then appeals to Allāh's own nature: "You are the Most Merciful of the merciful." He trusts that once Allāh knows your situation and you acknowledge His mercy, His response is inevitable. This du'ā contains no specific request — only a declaration of need and acknowledgment of Allāh's attribute. Allāh answered completely — removing the affliction and doubling his family.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr describes Ayyūb's trial as lasting eighteen years (other narrations say seven years and some months) during which he lost his wealth, his children, and his health, reportedly afflicted with a painful skin disease. What he never lost was his certainty in Allāh. His final supplication came not from despair but from a humble placing of his situation before Allāh — the most powerful form of prayer. The restoration doubled everything he had lost.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn notes that Allāh calls him "Our servant" (Abd-ana) — a title of immense honour. To be called Allāh's servant is the highest distinction. The phrase "dhikrā li-ūlī al-albāb — a reminder for people of understanding" tells us explicitly why his story is recorded: it is a lesson in patience and trust for those who think deeply. The story is not just history — it is instruction.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī explains the profound wisdom in Ayyūb's du'ā style: he did not say "O Allāh, please heal me." He said "I am suffering, and You are the Most Merciful." This form of supplication — presenting your state to Allāh and appealing to His attributes — is the most effective and most beloved du'ā. It is an expression of complete trust: I lay my condition before You, and I know what You will do because of who You are.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • 🤲 The most powerful du'ā presents your need and appeals to Allāh's attributes — not commands or bargains.
  • ⏳ Patience is not the absence of pain — Ayyūb felt every moment of his trial. Patience is remaining with Allāh through the pain.
  • 💰 Allāh doesn't just restore what is lost — He multiplies it as a mercy and proof.
  • 📚 Ayyūb's story is a "reminder for people of understanding" — Allāh intends it as a lesson, not just biography.
  • 🌟 The title "Our servant" (abdanā) is the greatest honour — servitude to Allāh is the highest human station.
38:71–85 The Creation of Adam — Iblīs's Refusal and Eternal Enmity
إِذْ قَالَ رَبُّكَ لِلْمَلَائِكَةِ إِنِّي خَالِقٌ بَشَرًا مِّن طِينٍ ﴿٧١﴾ فَإِذَا سَوَّيْتُهُ وَنَفَخْتُ فِيهِ مِن رُّوحِي فَقَعُوا لَهُ سَاجِدِينَ ﴿٧٢﴾ فَسَجَدَ الْمَلَائِكَةُ كُلُّهُمْ أَجْمَعُونَ ﴿٧٣﴾ إِلَّا إِبْلِيسَ اسْتَكْبَرَ وَكَانَ مِنَ الْكَافِرِينَ ﴿٧٤﴾ قَالَ أَنَا خَيْرٌ مِّنْهُ ۖ خَلَقْتَنِي مِن نَّارٍ وَخَلَقْتَهُ مِن طِينٍ ﴿٧٦﴾
"When your Lord said to the angels: 'Indeed, I will create a human being from clay. So when I have fashioned him and breathed into him of My [created] soul, fall down to him in prostration.' The angels prostrated — all of them entirely — except Iblīs; he was arrogant and became among the disbelievers. He said: 'I am better than him. You created me from fire and created him from clay.'" (38:71–76)
This scene is one of the most consequential in all of creation's history. Iblīs's logic — "I am made of fire, which is better than clay" — seems like elementary chemistry. But it is not a chemistry lesson; it is the origin of all pride. Iblīs used his own composition as a basis for superiority over Allāh's command. The moment you place your own reasoning above obedience to Allāh, you have taken the first step of Iblīs. His subsequent promise to mislead humanity is not from power — it is from wounded pride seeking company in its fall.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr explains that Iblīs was of the jinn, created from smokeless fire — yet he had worshipped Allāh with the angels for ages, earning a status among them. His fall was sudden because it was rooted in pride that had been lurking underneath his worship. This is the danger of worship without humility: a person can accumulate deeds for centuries and lose everything in one act of arrogance. The command to prostrate to Adam was not worship of Adam — it was a test of obedience to Allāh.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn notes that Iblīs's refusal, made in Allāh's direct presence with full knowledge, is the definition of kufr — knowing the truth and consciously rejecting it. His argument about fire over clay is a logical fallacy: obedience to Allāh's command does not require the created being to agree with the command's rationale. Reasoning your way around divine instruction is the path of Iblīs, not the path of faith.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī observes that Allāh exposed Iblīs's argument instantly — fire consuming clay is not inherently noble. And the divine spirit blown into Adam elevated clay above fire. The lesson is permanent: Allāh's choices are not subject to our understanding of material hierarchy. What Allāh honours is honoured; what He dismisses has no true value. Iblīs chose pride over obedience and lost both his station and his future.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • 😈 Pride (kibr) is the original sin — placing your logic above Allāh's command is the footstep of Iblīs.
  • ⚠️ Long worship without humility is vulnerable — arrogance can destroy centuries of devotion in an instant.
  • 🏅 Honour is defined by Allāh, not by material composition or worldly status.
  • 🛡️ Iblīs declared war on humanity — we must take the enmity seriously and guard our hearts.
  • 🌱 The divine spirit (rūḥ) breathed into Adam makes every human being inherently dignified — honour that in yourself and others.

Sūrah 39
الزُّمَر
Az-Zumar · "The Throngs / Groups"
75 Verses Total Meccan Juz 23: vv. 1–31

Named for the "throngs" (zumar) of people driven to Hell and then to Paradise on the Last Day, this surah is a masterpiece on sincere worship (ikhlāṣ), the danger of shirk, and the boundless mercy of Allāh. It contains the famous verse: "Do not despair of the mercy of Allāh." It opens Juz 24 at verse 32, but its first 31 verses — rich with theology — belong to Juz 23.

39:1–9 Sincere Worship — Ikhlāṣ Is Everything
تَنزِيلُ الْكِتَابِ مِنَ اللَّهِ الْعَزِيزِ الْحَكِيمِ ﴿١﴾ إِنَّا أَنزَلْنَا إِلَيْكَ الْكِتَابَ بِالْحَقِّ فَاعْبُدِ اللَّهَ مُخْلِصًا لَّهُ الدِّينَ ﴿٢﴾ أَلَا لِلَّهِ الدِّينُ الْخَالِصُ ﴿٣﴾ قُلْ إِنِّي أُمِرْتُ أَنْ أَعْبُدَ اللَّهَ مُخْلِصًا لَّهُ الدِّينَ ﴿١١﴾ أَمَّنْ هُوَ قَانِتٌ آنَاءَ اللَّيْلِ سَاجِدًا وَقَائِمًا يَحْذَرُ الْآخِرَةَ وَيَرْجُو رَحْمَةَ رَبِّهِ ﴿٩﴾
"The revelation of the Qur'ān is from Allāh, the Exalted in Might, the Wise. Indeed, We have sent down to you the Book in truth. So worship Allāh, sincere to Him in religion. Unquestionably, for Allāh is the pure religion... Is one who is devoutly obedient during periods of the night, prostrating and standing [in prayer], fearing the Hereafter and hoping for the mercy of his Lord [like one who does not]?" (39:1–3, 9)
The opening of Az-Zumar hammers one theme with relentless precision: ikhlāṣ — sincerity and purity of intention in worship. It is not enough to worship; the worship must be for Allāh alone, undiluted by desire for praise, status, or social approval. The surah then paints a portrait of the sincere believer: awake in the night, prostrating and standing, balancing fear of the Hereafter with hope for mercy. This person is not performing for an audience. They are alone with their Lord.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr explains that "Allāhu al-dīn al-khāliṣ" — pure religion belongs to Allāh alone — means that the only worship He accepts is that which is directed entirely to Him. The mushrikīn (polytheists) would claim their idols were merely intermediaries to Allāh, not worshipped in themselves. Allāh rejects this logic entirely: worship directed through another is worship given to another. The verse was also a direct refutation of intercession-based shirk.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn notes the rhetorical question in verse 9 as a sharp contrast: the person who prays in the depths of night, alone, balancing hope and fear — is this person the same as one who is heedless? The question answers itself. The night prayer (qiyām al-layl) is highlighted because it has no social reward — no one sees it, no one praises you for it. It is the purest test of sincerity.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī beautifully explains the balance in verse 9: "fearing the Hereafter and hoping for the mercy of his Lord." True worship lives between these two — fear alone leads to despair, hope alone leads to complacency. The servant who combines both is the one whose worship is complete. The night prayer is a private conversation — no witnesses, no reward in this world, only Allāh.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • 🎯 Ikhlāṣ — sincerity — is the soul of worship. Without it, the most elaborate acts are empty.
  • 🌙 Night prayer is the gold standard of sincerity — no audience, no applause, only Allāh.
  • ⚖️ Worship lives between fear and hope — both are necessary, neither alone is sufficient.
  • 🚫 Intercession through false objects is still shirk — Allāh alone receives our worship directly.
  • 📖 The Qur'ān is sent "with truth" — its purpose is to purify our relationship with Allāh.
39:17–26 Two Groups — The One Who Avoids Ṭāghūt and the One Who Follows Whims
وَالَّذِينَ اجْتَنَبُوا الطَّاغُوتَ أَن يَعْبُدُوهَا وَأَنَابُوا إِلَى اللَّهِ لَهُمُ الْبُشْرَىٰ ﴿١٧﴾ أَفَمَن شَرَحَ اللَّهُ صَدْرَهُ لِلْإِسْلَامِ فَهُوَ عَلَىٰ نُورٍ مِّن رَّبِّهِ ۚ فَوَيْلٌ لِّلْقَاسِيَةِ قُلُوبُهُم مِّن ذِكْرِ اللَّهِ ﴿٢٢﴾ اللَّهُ نَزَّلَ أَحْسَنَ الْحَدِيثِ كِتَابًا مُّتَشَابِهًا مَّثَانِيَ تَقْشَعِرُّ مِنْهُ جُلُودُ الَّذِينَ يَخْشَوْنَ رَبَّهُمْ ثُمَّ تَلِينُ جُلُودُهُمْ وَقُلُوبُهُمْ إِلَىٰ ذِكْرِ اللَّهِ ﴿٢٣﴾
"Those who avoided ṭāghūt, lest they worship it, and turned back to Allāh — for them is glad tidings... So is one whose breast Allāh has expanded to [accept] Islām, and he is upon a light from his Lord, like one whose heart is hardened? Woe to those whose hearts are hardened against the remembrance of Allāh... Allāh has sent down the best statement: a consistent Book wherein is reiteration. The skins shiver from it of those who fear their Lord; then their skins and their hearts relax at the remembrance of Allāh." (39:17, 22–23)
Verse 23 is among the most beautiful descriptions of the Qur'ān's effect on the believer in all of scripture. When the fearful heart encounters the words of Allāh, the skin shudders — a physical, involuntary response to meeting something greater than yourself. Then, the skin and heart soften — the shudder gives way to calm. This is the Qur'ān's two-part effect: first awe, then peace. If you read the Qur'ān without feeling either, it is not the Qur'ān's fault — it is a sign that the heart needs tending.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr explains "shraḥa Allāhu ṣadrahu — Allāh has opened his chest for Islām" as a divine gift: the capacity to receive guidance is itself from Allāh. No one can force their heart open — it is Allāh who opens it. The hardened heart (qāsiyah) is its opposite — closed, sealed, like rock that water cannot penetrate. The difference between the two is not intelligence or social status; it is Allāh's will operating through the servant's choice to turn toward or away from Him.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn explains "mutashābihan mathāniya — consistent and oft-repeated" as a description of the Qur'ān's coherence: its parts confirm each other, its themes repeat and reinforce. Unlike human writing that contradicts itself over time, the Qur'ān repeats its truths from different angles without contradiction. The repetition (mathānī) is not redundancy — it is the Qur'ān teaching the way a master teacher does: returning to the core truth again and again until it settles in the heart.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī reflects on the sequence: shiver then soften. The shiver (iqshi'rār) is the trembling of a heart that recognises its smallness before Allāh's greatness. The softening (talīn) is the peace that comes from knowing that this Great Lord is also the Most Merciful. Both responses are signs of a living, healthy heart. A heart that does neither has been numbed by sin and heedlessness.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • 📖 The Qur'ān has a physical effect on believing hearts — shivering then softening. Seek this experience.
  • 💡 An open chest (sharḥ al-ṣadr) for Islām is a gift from Allāh — ask Him for it in your du'ā.
  • 🪨 A hardened heart is the most dangerous spiritual condition — dhikr is its remedy.
  • 🔄 The Qur'ān repeats themes because hearts need repetition — don't rush past familiar verses.
  • 🚫 Avoiding ṭāghūt (everything worshipped besides Allāh) is paired with turning to Allāh — both are necessary.
39:29–31 The Parable of the Slave — One Master vs. Many Masters
ضَرَبَ اللَّهُ مَثَلًا رَّجُلًا فِيهِ شُرَكَاءُ مُتَشَاكِسُونَ وَرَجُلًا سَلَمًا لِّرَجُلٍ هَلْ يَسْتَوِيَانِ مَثَلًا ۚ الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ ۚ بَلْ أَكْثَرُهُمْ لَا يَعْلَمُونَ ﴿٢٩﴾ إِنَّكَ مَيِّتٌ وَإِنَّهُم مَّيِّتُونَ ﴿٣٠﴾ ثُمَّ إِنَّكُمْ يَوْمَ الْقِيَامَةِ عِندَ رَبِّكُمْ تَخْتَصِمُونَ ﴿٣١﴾
"Allāh presents an example: a man belonging to quarrelling partners and another belonging entirely to one man — are they equal in comparison? Praise be to Allāh! But most of them do not know. Indeed you are to die, and indeed they are to die. Then indeed you, on the Day of Resurrection, will dispute before your Lord." (39:29–31)
One of the simplest and most devastating parables in the Qur'ān. Imagine a slave torn between multiple quarrelling owners — each pulling him in a different direction, each demanding loyalty, each unsatisfied. Now imagine a slave with one kind, clear master — focused, purposeful, at peace. The parable of shirk versus Tawḥīd is this simple. Worshipping multiple things — money, status, desires, people — tears you apart. Worshipping One Allāh gives you coherence, direction, and peace. Then verse 30 lands like a stone: everyone dies. The dispute that began in this life continues on Judgment Day.
Tafsīr Ibn Kathīr

Ibn Kathīr explains that the "quarrelling partners" represent the false objects of worship — idols, desires, wealth — each making competing demands and none truly satisfying. The person who serves many masters is scattered, anxious, and ultimately serves none well. The person with one master has clarity. The Qur'ān uses this human, relatable experience to illuminate the theology of Tawḥīd: monotheism is not just theologically correct — it is psychologically healthy.

Tafsīr Al-Jalālayn

Al-Jalālayn explains "mutashākisūn — quarrelling, contentious" as describing the inner conflict of the person in shirk: torn between competing loyalties, unable to satisfy any fully, perpetually anxious. This is the psychological reality of every person who has not unified their devotion to Allāh. Peace of heart is not a side benefit of Tawḥīd — it is its natural fruit.

Tafsīr As-Sa'dī

As-Sa'dī notes that after the parable, Allāh abruptly reminds the Prophet and all readers: "You will die, and they will die." This is not pessimism — it is orientation. The debate about Tawḥīd is not an abstract philosophical exercise. It has eternal consequences. Those who rejected in this life will face those they rejected alongside on Judgment Day — and the dispute will be settled there, finally and permanently.

🔑 Key Lessons
  • ☮️ Tawḥīd brings inner peace — serving One Master is psychologically liberating, not constraining.
  • 😰 Shirk — divided devotion — creates inner chaos, anxiety, and perpetual dissatisfaction.
  • ⏳ Death is the great equaliser — the Prophet ﷺ and his opponents all die. What matters is what comes after.
  • ⚖️ Every worldly dispute unresolved by truth will be settled on Judgment Day — no injustice is forgotten.
  • 🎯 Ask yourself: how many "masters" are dividing your attention? Unify your heart toward Allāh.

✨ Juz 23 — Overarching Themes

☝️

Tawḥīd — Divine Oneness

From the oath of the angels to the parable of the slave, every surah returns to the absolute uniqueness and oneness of Allāh. Shirk is dissected and exposed — intellectually, emotionally, and spiritually.

🌱

Resurrection & Judgment

Yā-Sīn's cosmic signs, Aṣ-Ṣāffāt's vivid scenes of the Last Day, and Az-Zumar's throngs — the certainty of resurrection is argued, illustrated, and made viscerally real.

🕊️

Prophetic Stories

Ibrāhīm, Ismā'īl, Yūnus, Dāwūd, Sulaymān, and Ayyūb — each prophet faces unique trials, each models the response Allāh loves: surrender, patience, and return to Him.

💎

Ikhlāṣ — Sincerity

Az-Zumar opens with an extended call to pure, sincere worship. Religion without sincerity is performance. Allāh accepts only what is given for His sake alone, without partner or show.

😈

The Danger of Pride

Iblīs's refusal, the Quraysh's rejection, and the stories of those who declined truth — all share one root: kibr (arrogance). The greatest enemy of faith is pride disguised as logic.

🤲

Tawbah & Mercy

Dāwūd's immediate repentance, Yūnus's du'ā in the whale, and Ayyūb's humble presentation of his need — Juz 23 is a guide to returning to Allāh and trusting His mercy without condition.

🌙

Night Worship & Remembrance

Az-Zumar praises those who stand in the night in prayer. The Qur'ān's physical effect — shivering then softening — is described as the hallmark of a living heart connected to its Lord.

📖

The Qur'ān Itself

The Juz opens with Yā-Sīn — the heart of the Qur'ān — and closes with Az-Zumar's description of the Qur'ān as "the best of speech." This Juz is, in part, the Qur'ān speaking about itself.

وَاللَّهُ يَهْدِي مَن يَشَاءُ إِلَىٰ صِرَاطٍ مُّسْتَقِيمٍ "And Allāh guides whom He wills to a straight path." (2:213)
